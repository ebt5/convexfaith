### The New Absorbing Barriers

Sin is our biggest impediment. Sin represents the absorbing barriers that threaten to pull us into a narrow, deceitfully satisfying life. The modern world provides a particular challenge in this regard, and it is worth pausing to understand why.

Technology has an interesting bias embedded in it. Technology relies on engineering. Engineering relies on logic, and logic requires adjacent ideas to build upon each other. This means our engineered technologies represent a tiny subset of what is possible—and more importantly, they are naive relative to the full breadth and depth of the problems they attempt to address. Any deployed technology targets one or only a handful of problems, and by so doing creates consequences far beyond the narrow problem it was built to solve. Consider the automobile as an improvement on the horse—targeting the problems of velocity, maintenance, and manure. Besides largely solving those problems, automobiles polluted the environment and completely transformed our cities from walkable, human-scaled places into sprawling zones designed around machines rather than people. This is not to say automobiles were a mistake. It is to demonstrate the naivete inherent in all technology and the unexpected problems that arise alongside the solutions technology provides.

There are two effects we must confront as technology marches forward and accelerates.

The first is the creation of new absorbing barriers. Technology excels at exploiting low-order satiation—the immediate, engineerable satisfactions—because these are the kind that can be optimized for. The satisfactions that yield to measurement and iteration: clicks, calories, dopamine hits, frictionless transactions. But depth does not yield to measurement, which means technology is structurally blind to it. In solving for what it can see, technology actively displaces what it cannot. Every app engineered to capture attention, every algorithm tuned to deliver frictionless pleasure, every system that removes difficulty without understanding why difficulty existed, every processed food that optimizes for taste and consistency at the expense of nobody knows what—these are new walls that, once you hit them, are extraordinarily hard to escape. They don't look like walls. They look like comfort. And critically, they don't merely prevent you from going deeper. They persuade you that there is no deeper to go—that the satiation they provide is the thing you were always looking for. We slouch into thinness rather than compound into depth.

The second, perhaps more difficult problem, is that we forget the depth we used to have—both as individuals and as cultures.

A puzzling pattern emerged along the American frontier that troubled the finest minds of the eighteenth century. Thousands of white settlers—men, women, and children—were taken captive by Native American tribes. This was common enough that a whole literary genre, the captivity narrative, arose around it. But the disquieting part was what happened when these captives were offered their freedom. They refused to come home. Not some of them—a striking and well-documented majority. Benjamin Franklin noted the phenomenon directly in his 1753 letter to Peter Collinson: "When an Indian child has been brought up among us, taught our language and habituated to our customs, yet if he goes to see his relations and makes one Indian ramble with them, there is no persuading him ever to return." J. Hector St. John de Crèvecoeur, writing in *Letters from an American Farmer*, found it no less baffling: "There must be in their social bond something singularly captivating, and far superior to anything to be boasted of among us." Colonial authorities organized formal return expeditions. In some cases, captives had to be physically restrained. They wept when separated from their adopted tribes. The migration ran almost entirely in one direction.

What did tribal life have that colonial society did not? Not comfort. Not security. Not material advantage of any kind. What it had was a total claim on a person—a web of obligation, belonging, loyalty, and shared meaning so complete that individual comfort became beside the point. Franklin and Crèvecoeur were honest enough to name the indictment this implied: that a society organized around material accumulation and rational self-interest had purchased its comforts at the cost of something that turns out to matter more.

But the captivity evidence carries a harder implication that should not be dissolved too quickly. This was not merely an indictment of Enlightenment materialism. By the mid-eighteenth century, Christianity had been the dominant shaping force in European civilization for well over a thousand years. These colonists were not pagans. They were baptized, churchgoing, scripture-literate people—in many cases deeply earnest ones. And still, the depth was gone. Given a choice between their Christian society and a pre-Christian tribal one, they chose the tribal one. This is not an argument against Christianity. It is a more uncomfortable argument: that doctrine can be preserved while the eros that gives doctrine its life quietly drains away. That Christianity's institutional presence is not a sufficient guarantee of the depth Christianity is supposed to produce. The problem was not the gospel. The problem was what had been lost in transmission.

The Orthodox theologian Timothy Patitsas gives this missing thing a name: good eros. In his account (developed in *The Ethics of Beauty*), good eros is not desire in its debased sense but desire in its proper one—a provocation toward depth, beauty, and life that carries the force of erotic passion without being sexual. This is what distinguishes it from every other single motivation that a person might organize a life around: wealth, comfort, status, intellectual superiority, travel, technology, ease. All of those motivations, pursued far enough, flatten and thin existence. They discharge desire and leave you smaller. Good eros compounds. The more you pursue it, the more there is. Each layer of genuine depth opens onto another; the world does not shrink but expands. This is why Patitsas likens it to a kind of non-naive technology—it has the drive and urgency and transformative power of technology, but aimed precisely at what technology is structurally blind to. It is what made tribal belonging feel like an answer that material civilization could not match. Not because material civilization lacked resources, but because it was optimizing for the wrong thing.

This distinction matters enormously for how we understand Christ's invitation. It is not primarily a discipline. It is not a cost to pay for future reward. It is an eros toward depth and building—a provocation toward what is actually there, once the barriers stop blocking the view. And this is precisely what modern absorbing barriers suppress. They do not eliminate eros altogether—that would be too obvious. They redirect it. They replace the provocation toward depth with the provocation toward satiation, training us to expect that the answer to desire is the discharge of desire rather than its deepening. Each discharged desire leaves the world a little smaller than it found it.

None of this points toward a return to tribal life. That possibility needs to be named and set aside directly, because the romanticism it invites is not honest. Eight billion people are alive today largely because of modern medicine, water treatment, and agricultural infrastructure that did not exist a century ago. Progress is not a mistake to be undone. It is a fundamental human drive and, at this scale, a practical necessity. The problem is not progress itself. The problem is that in pursuing progress we have learned to commodify people—to optimize human beings the same way we optimize supply chains, attention spans, and nutrient profiles. The task is to commodify things, automate things, build at scale—without commodifying people in the process. To achieve the material gains without thinning the humanity that makes those gains worth having.

This is the challenge of the S-curve. Every system that grows—organisms, economies, technologies, civilizations—tends to follow a curve that rises steeply, then reaches a second inflection point where growth goes concave. Past that inflection, returns diminish, vitality drains, and the curve bends toward terminal decline. What absorbing barriers do, at civilizational scale, is accelerate the approach of that second inflection. The thinning of depth, the erosion of meaning, the replacement of good eros with its counterfeit—these are the mechanisms by which the curve bends. The task is to postpone or destroy that second inflection. To keep building. To keep the curve convex.

The Restoration and the Book of Mormon are specifically engaged with these dynamics. Lehi's discourse in 2 Nephi 2 is not merely a theological argument about opposition and agency. It is a guide to ratcheting toward creation and depth at civilizational scale—an anatomy of the mechanics by which freedom either compounds into joy or discharges into captivity. The opposition Lehi describes is not abstract. It is the structural condition of a world in which good eros is possible at all: you cannot pursue depth in a universe that offers no resistance, just as you cannot build on a surface that dissolves under your weight. This is why the Restoration matters not as a religious curiosity but as a civilizational intervention—one that names the mechanism, restores the eros, and points toward a curve that does not have to bend.
