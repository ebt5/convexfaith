### The New Absorbing Barriers

Sin is our biggest impediment. Sin represents the absorbing barriers that threaten to pull us into a narrow, deceitfully satisfying life. The modern world provides a particular challenge in this regard, and it is worth pausing to understand why.

Technology has an interesting bias embedded in it. Technology relies on engineering. Engineering relies on logic, and logic requires adjacent ideas to build upon each other. This means our engineered technologies represent a tiny subset of what is possible—and more importantly, they are naive relative to the full breadth and depth of the problems they attempt to address. Any deployed technology targets one or only a handful of problems, and by so doing creates consequences far beyond the narrow problem it was built to solve. Consider the automobile as an improvement on the horse—targeting the problems of velocity, maintenance, and manure. Besides largely solving those problems, automobiles polluted the environment and completely transformed our cities from walkable, human-scaled places into sprawling zones designed around machines rather than people. This is not to say automobiles were a mistake. It is to demonstrate the naivete inherent in all technology and the unexpected problems that arise alongside the solutions technology provides.

There are two effects we must confront as technology marches forward and accelerates.

The first is the creation of new absorbing barriers. Technology excels at exploiting low-order satiation—the immediate, engineerable satisfactions—because these are the kind that can be optimized for. The satisfactions that yield to measurement and iteration: clicks, calories, dopamine hits, frictionless transactions. But depth does not yield to measurement, which means technology is structurally blind to it. In solving for what it can see, technology actively displaces what it cannot. Every app engineered to capture attention, every algorithm tuned to deliver frictionless pleasure, every system that removes difficulty without understanding why difficulty existed, every processed food that optimizes for taste and consistency at the expense of nobody knows what—these are new walls that, once you hit them, are extraordinarily hard to escape. They don't look like walls. They look like comfort. And critically, they don't merely prevent you from going deeper. They persuade you that there is no deeper to go—that the satiation they provide is the thing you were always looking for. We slouch into thinness rather than compound into depth.

The second, perhaps more difficult problem, is that we forget the depth we used to have—both as individuals and as cultures.

A puzzling pattern emerged along the American frontier that troubled the finest minds of the eighteenth century. Thousands of white settlers—men, women, and children—were taken captive by Native American tribes. This was common enough that a whole literary genre, the captivity narrative, arose around it. But the disquieting part was what happened when these captives were offered their freedom. They refused to come home. Not some of them—a striking and well-documented majority. Benjamin Franklin noted the phenomenon directly in his 1753 letter to Peter Collinson: "When an Indian child has been brought up among us, taught our language and habituated to our customs, yet if he goes to see his relations and makes one Indian ramble with them, there is no persuading him ever to return." J. Hector St. John de Crèvecoeur, writing in *Letters from an American Farmer*, found it no less baffling: "There must be in their social bond something singularly captivating, and far superior to anything to be boasted of among us." Colonial authorities organized formal return expeditions. Captives were physically restrained. They wept at separation. Meanwhile, Native Americans taken into colonial households would seize the first opportunity to slip back into the forest. The migration ran almost entirely in one direction.

What did tribal life have that colonial society did not? Not comfort. Not security. Not material advantage of any kind. What it had was a total claim on a person—a web of obligation, belonging, loyalty, and shared meaning so complete that individual comfort became beside the point. You knew exactly who you were, exactly what was expected of you, and exactly who would grieve if you died.

Here is the indictment that Franklin and Crèvecoeur were honest enough to register but not quite able to absorb: several centuries of Christianity had, at the civilizational scale of colonial European society, apparently failed to produce that depth broadly or universally. This is not an argument against Christianity. It is an observation about how difficult it is to sustain depth at scale—and how a civilization organized around material accumulation and rational self-interest can go formally Christian while quietly evacuating the interior life that Christianity is supposed to cultivate. The captivity phenomenon did not merely indict colonial manners. It indicted something structural.

And yet the answer cannot be to reverse the process. We cannot return to tribal life—not because it was inferior, but because it is simply impossible at modern scale. There are eight billion people alive, the vast majority of whom are alive only because of the agricultural, medical, and logistical infrastructure that industrial civilization built. Insulin. Vaccines. Water treatment. Surgery. The grain supply. These systems do not scale down to village life. They require the civilization that produced them—which means they require the division of labor, the specialization, the commerce, the complexity, the institutions, all of it. We depend on progress not merely as an aspiration but as a physical precondition for the survival of billions of people now in existence.

More than that: progress is a fundamental drive of mankind. We want to know more, build more, go further. That drive is not a pathology to be cured. It is part of what it means to be the kind of beings who can leave the Garden. The question is not whether to pursue progress but how to pursue it without commodifying humanity in the process—without automating and optimizing people the way we automate and optimize supply chains. We can and should commodify things. We should not commodify people. We can and should automate processes. We should not automate the human relationships and human making that give life its texture. The task is to achieve scale and progress without thinning humanity itself—to keep the second inflection of the civilizational S-curve from arriving. Every S-curve has a point where the rate of growth peaks and begins to diminish—where the curve goes concave. The question for any civilization is whether it can compound forward into the next S-curve before the current one exhausts itself, or whether it plateaus and declines. The absorbing barriers of thinness—the slow erosion of depth by comfort—are exactly what collapses a civilization's S-curve from the inside.

This is the problem that the Orthodox theologian Timothy Patitsas, in his book *The Ethics of Beauty*, names with more precision than almost anyone writing today. What tribal life had, what colonial Christianity failed to broadly restore, and what modern technology systematically destroys—Patitsas calls good eros. Good eros is not desire in its debased sense. It is desire in its proper and original one: the provocation that pulls the pursuer not toward satiation but toward depth, beauty, and life. It has the force of erotic passion—the urgency, the orientation, the willingness to sacrifice—but unlike erotic attraction, or the pull of wealth, or the pleasure of status, or the comfort of ease, it does not terminate. Most motivations are self-consuming: you pursue the thing, you obtain it, the desire discharges, and the world is a little smaller than it was before. Good eros is different. The more you pursue it, the more there is to pursue. It compounds. It opens. The person who follows it deeply enough finds themselves capable of things they could not have imagined from where they started.

This is why Christ's invitation is not, at its core, a rulebook or a discipline. It is an eros. It is the provocation toward depth and building and beauty that, once felt, reorganizes everything else. The Atonement does not merely remove punishment. It removes the absorbing barriers that prevent good eros from functioning—the concavity that makes every failure terminal, the spiritual death that waits at the bottom of every moral stumble. With that barrier lifted, variance becomes productive again. The person can afford to reach, to risk, to commit, to love at depth. Righteousness stops being a cost to pay and becomes an attraction: the natural direction of a soul that has been shown what is actually worth wanting. The counterfeit eros of technology trains us to expect that the answer to desire is the discharge of desire rather than its deepening. Good eros runs the other direction entirely.

What is striking—and what makes the Book of Mormon's opening chapters something more than theology—is that Lehi and Nephi are working on precisely this problem. Second Nephi chapter two is not merely a philosophical treatise on the Fall. It is a guide to how moral agents, operating in history at scale, can ratchet toward creation and depth rather than toward thinness and dissolution. It is a manual for keeping the curve convex. Lehi knows that Adam and Eve's departure from the Garden was the opening move in a project, not a catastrophe to be managed. He knows that the absorbing barriers—death, the fall, the law's condemnation—have been addressed. He is trying to tell his sons what that means for how they are supposed to live now: not cautiously, not defensively, not in compliance with a system designed to limit damage, but with the full urgency of beings who have been shown the shape of the possible and are being asked to help build it.

Where there is no vision the people perish. This was always true, but it is more dangerous now because the barriers are subtler and the forgetting is faster. The absorbing barriers of the modern world don't merely trap us—they erase our memory that anything beyond them exists. They flatten our vision of what a life can be until the flatness itself feels natural. Family dinner is one example from my own life. My own family dinners are less frequent and thinner than those I grew up with, and hearing stories from my father I know that mine were less frequent and thinner than those he had as a child. That thinning is not accidental. It is what absorbing barriers do over generations—not through malice but through the relentless logic of optimization for what is measurable at the expense of what is not. The recovery is not nostalgia and it is not retreat. It is the restoration of good eros—the recovered ability to want the right things, to feel the provocation toward depth rather than satiation, and to find the depth, at last, responsive.
