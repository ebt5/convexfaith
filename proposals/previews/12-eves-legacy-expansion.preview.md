Eve's Legacy

We return, finally, to Eve.

Eve invoked the final separation—the last broken symmetry that made moral agency possible. But what Eve saw, and what her decision implies, is that separation was never the end. The same boldness that led her to break the symmetry of the Garden is the boldness required to enter covenant. She did not eat the fruit so that mankind could remain scattered in moral confusion. She ate it so that mankind could, through the long process of learning to act rather than merely be acted upon, eventually be assembled into something that could not have existed in the Garden's protected simplicity.

The sealed marriage, the covenant community, the body of Christ—these are Eve's inheritance. They are the assembly phase of the creation she initiated. She broke the symmetry so that, in the fullness of time, Christ could gather all things together in one—not destroying the distinctions the Fall introduced, but bringing them to their intended fruition in a new and higher unity.

The arc of creation is: separation, that there might be agency. Agency, that there might be covenant. Covenant, that there might be assembly. Assembly, that there might be joy.

Adam fell that man might be, and men are, that they might have joy.

Each movement in that arc required the previous one to be real. The separation had to be a genuine break—not a rehearsed exit, not a trial run. The moral differentiation of the Fall had to carry genuine consequence, or there would have been nothing to differentiate and no one capable of choosing. The Atonement had to reach all the way down to where we actually are, not where a cleaner version of us might have been—which is why it required Christ, and not an amendment to the original terms. The absorbing barrier had to be removed from inside the state we occupy. And the assembly—the covenant, the sealed relationship, the gathered body—has to be freely chosen by agents who have already proven, through the cost of their choosing, that their alignment is real. You cannot assemble what was never genuinely scattered.

Eve perhaps knew this. Before there was a rulebook she looked out at the dimensionality of a moral world—its beauty, its stakes, its staggering potential—and she chose to enter it. She did not compute. She acted from the deepest thing she knew, from what we might call faith, and trusted that something larger would catch what she dropped.

It did. It does. It will.

We live in a post-Christ world. The Atonement has been performed. What that means is not that suffering has been explained or that the stakes have been lowered, but that the barrier between where we are and where the arc ends has been permanently removed. We are not waiting for the mechanism to be invented. We are living in the energy of its shock wave, moving through a world where the final assembly is not hypothetical but inevitable—and where every act of genuine covenant, every moment of choosing to act rather than be acted upon, is already part of it.

The pilot who understands aerodynamics is not constrained by physics—she is freed by it. Compliance with natural law does not narrow what is possible; it opens the sky. Alignment with Christ works the same way: it is alignment with the structure of reality, and reality is generous to those who move with it rather than against it. To follow Christ is not to contract your life into a narrower corridor but to expand it—to trade the illusion of freedom that comes from drifting wherever employers, governments, and fear push you, for the genuine agency of someone who knows where she is going and why. The question is not whether to exit the Garden. You have already eaten the fruit. The question is whether you will walk out on your own terms—whether you will accept the weight of agency in moral space, choose to act rather than be acted upon, and let that choosing, again and again, assemble you into something that moves, under all the noise and uncertainty, toward what is good. Do you wish to exit the Garden?
