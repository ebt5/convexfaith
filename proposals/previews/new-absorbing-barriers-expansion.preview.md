### The New Absorbing Barriers

Sin is our biggest impediment. Sin represents the absorbing barriers that threaten to pull us into a narrow, deceitfully satisfying life. The modern world provides a particular challenge in this regard, and it is worth pausing to understand why.

Technology has an interesting bias embedded in it. Technology relies on engineering. Engineering relies on logic, and logic requires adjacent ideas to build upon each other. This means our engineered technologies represent a tiny subset of what is possible—and more importantly, they are naive relative to the full breadth and depth of the problems they attempt to address. Any deployed technology targets one or only a handful of problems, and by so doing creates consequences far beyond the narrow problem it was built to solve. Consider the automobile as an improvement on the horse—targeting the problems of velocity, maintenance, and manure. Besides largely solving those problems, automobiles polluted the environment and completely transformed our cities from walkable, human-scaled places into sprawling zones designed around machines rather than people. This is not to say automobiles were a mistake. It is to demonstrate the naivete inherent in all technology and the unexpected problems that arise alongside the solutions technology provides.

There are two effects we must confront as technology marches forward and accelerates.

The first is the creation of new absorbing barriers. Technology excels at exploiting low-order satiation—the immediate, engineerable satisfactions—because these are the kind that can be optimized for. The satisfactions that yield to measurement and iteration: clicks, calories, dopamine hits, frictionless transactions. But depth does not yield to measurement, which means technology is structurally blind to it. In solving for what it can see, technology actively displaces what it cannot. Every app engineered to capture attention, every algorithm tuned to deliver frictionless pleasure, every system that removes difficulty without understanding why difficulty existed, every processed food that optimizes for taste and consistency at the expense of nobody knows what—these are new walls that, once you hit them, are extraordinarily hard to escape. They don't look like walls. They look like comfort. And critically, they don't merely prevent you from going deeper. They persuade you that there is no deeper to go—that the satiation they provide is the thing you were always looking for. We slouch into thinness rather than compound into depth.

The second, perhaps more difficult problem, is that we forget the depth we used to have—both as individuals and as cultures.

A puzzling pattern emerged along the American frontier that troubled the finest minds of the eighteenth century. Thousands of white settlers—men, women, and children—were taken captive by Native American tribes. This was common enough that a whole literary genre, the captivity narrative, arose around it. But the disquieting part was what happened when these captives were offered their freedom. They refused to come home. Not some of them—a striking and well-documented majority. Benjamin Franklin noted the phenomenon directly in his 1753 letter to Peter Collinson: "When an Indian child has been brought up among us, taught our language and habituated to our customs, yet if he goes to see his relations and makes one Indian ramble with them, there is no persuading him ever to return. And an Instance of a Whiteman that had lived a long time among the Indians, but who, when ransomed by his friends and restored to us and to his civilization, took the first good opportunity of escaping back into the woods from which we had with so much care and expense rescued him, will not be singular." J. Hector St. John de Crèvecoeur, writing in *Letters from an American Farmer*, found it no less baffling: "There must be in their social bond something singularly captivating, and far superior to anything to be boasted of among us." Colonial authorities, missionaries, and military commanders organized formal return expeditions. In some cases, captives had to be physically restrained. They wept when separated from their adopted tribes. Meanwhile, Native Americans taken into colonial households and educated in white schools would seize the first opportunity to slip back into the forest. The migration ran almost entirely in one direction.

What did tribal life have that colonial society did not? Not comfort. Not security. Not material advantage of any kind. What it had was a total claim on a person—a web of obligation, belonging, loyalty, and shared meaning so complete that individual comfort became beside the point. You knew exactly who you were, exactly what was expected of you, and exactly who would grieve if you died. Franklin and Crèvecoeur were honest enough to name the indictment this implied: that a society organized around material accumulation and rational self-interest had purchased its comforts at the cost of something that turns out to matter more. I would conjecture that twenty-first century Western culture is even more devoid of that something than the eighteenth-century colonial culture that disturbed Franklin so deeply.

The Orthodox theologian Timothy Patitsas gives this missing thing a name: good eros. In his account, good eros is not desire in its debased sense but desire in its proper one—the provocation that aims us toward depth, beauty, and life rather than toward satiation. It is the feeling that existence is worth compounding, that the world contains more than you have yet discovered, that the next layer is worth reaching for. Good eros is what makes a person willing to endure difficulty, to take on obligation, to remain in relationships that demand something from them. It is the opposite of thinness. The tribal captives felt it. Colonial society had lost it and replaced it with what Patitsas would call the counterfeit: desire that terminates at satisfaction and therefore shrinks the world rather than opening it.

This is precisely what the absorbing barriers of modern technology are engineered to suppress. They do not suppress eros altogether—that would be too obvious. They redirect it. They replace the provocation toward depth with provocation toward satiation, training us to expect that the answer to desire is the discharge of desire rather than its deepening. Each discharged desire leaves the world a little smaller than it found it.

Where there is no vision the people perish. This was always true, but it is more dangerous now because the barriers are subtler and the forgetting is faster. The absorbing barriers of the modern world don't merely trap us—they erase our memory that anything beyond them exists. They flatten our vision of what a life can be until the flatness itself feels natural. Family dinner is one example in my own life. My own family dinners are less frequent and thinner than those I grew up with, and hearing stories from my father I know that mine were less frequent and thinner than those he had as a child. That thinning is not accidental. It is what absorbing barriers do over generations—not through malice but through the relentless logic of optimization for what is measurable at the expense of what is not.

This is why the Atonement matters in ways that go beyond the forensic. Lehi told his sons that men are that they might have joy, and Paul told the Philippians to think on things that are true, honest, just, pure, lovely, and of good report. Neither was offering a discipline. Both were pointing at something they had seen—a reality so alive that encountering it rearranges what you want. Christ's work is not primarily the removal of punishment. It is the restoration of good eros. When we come into alignment with moral reality—when sin's absorbing barriers lose their grip—we begin to see again. The world fills back in. Opportunity for building, for depth, for beauty reappears. Righteousness stops being a cost to pay and becomes an attraction, a direction, a pull toward something worth moving toward. The Kingdom is not a destination we endure our way into. It is the recovered ability to want the right things—to feel the provocation toward depth rather than satiation, and to find the depth, at last, responsive.
