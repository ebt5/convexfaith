# Proposal: The Fork and the Depth

*For insertion in Chapter 4, after "The New Absorbing Barriers" section, before "Shape Over Calculation"*

---

## The Fork

Technology does not accelerate everyone equally.

This is the fact that every prior acceleration has demonstrated, and which the current one will demonstrate more sharply than any that preceded it. The printing press did not merely speed up communication—it forked Europe. Those who could read obtained a disproportionate share of everything that followed: wealth, influence, doctrinal authority, scientific participation, political power. The fork was not malicious. It was structural. The tool distributed its benefits along the axis of the capability it amplified, and because that capability was not uniformly distributed, the tool's benefits were not uniformly distributed either. The gap that opened between the literate and the illiterate was not a gap in work ethic or intelligence. It was a gap in access to a new kind of leverage.

AI is not merely a faster printing press. It is leverage applied to cognition itself—to the capacity that previously set human beings apart from their tools. And unlike the printing press, which created a single fork (those who could read versus those who could not), AI threatens to create a recursive one. Because the tool amplifies thinking, those who use it well think faster and better, which makes them more capable of using it even better, which makes them faster still. The compounding is not linear. The curve bends. And as the curve bends far enough, the people on the accelerating side of it do not merely outpace those on the other side. They begin to inhabit a different kind of time—a different cadence of discovery, accumulation, and possibility—than those they leave behind.

This is not a speculation about a distant future. It is a description of a dynamic already underway. But the question before us is not economic or political, though it will manifest in those registers. The question is evolutionary in the deepest sense: whether the acceleration might produce, over generations, a fork so deep that what emerges on either side is no longer recognizably the same kind of being. History offers precedents. Not all of them are reassuring. Evolution has forked species that once shared a common ancestor into organisms so divergent that they can no longer interbreed, no longer communicate, no longer recognize each other as kin. The fork is always biological in retrospect. But it begins as a difference in relationship to environment—in which pressures a group responds to, and which capabilities it develops in response.

The question is whether this fork can be avoided. And if not avoided—whether something can be done about which side of it your community lands on.

## Why Depth Is the Answer, and Why That Is Counterintuitive

The obvious answer is capability. Get better at using the tools. Learn to prompt, to program, to leverage. This is not wrong—it is simply insufficient, and insufficient in a specific way that the framework of this book is positioned to explain.

What makes the accelerating side of the fork accelerating is not merely the acquisition of new capabilities. It is the compounding of those capabilities over time. A person who acquires a capability and does not compound it falls behind even as they seem to be advancing. Compounding requires a second thing that capability alone cannot supply: the asymmetric relationship with uncertainty that we have been calling convexity. The person who compounds is not merely the person who learns faster. It is the person whose errors are bounded and whose gains are open—the person whose trajectory, across the full distribution of outcomes over a life, bends upward rather than flattening into the saturating plateau of an S-curve that has exhausted itself.

And what produces this convex relationship with uncertainty is not skill. It is depth.

Depth—what Patitsas calls good eros, what Lehi calls acting rather than being acted upon, what the Sermon on the Mount describes from the inside—is the quality of a soul that is genuinely provoked by reality. Not performing provocation. Not managing the appearance of curiosity. Actually drawn outward and upward by what it encounters. This orientation produces a fundamentally different relationship with failure. For the deep person, error is data. It is interesting. It carries information about the actual shape of the world. For the thin person—the person whose desires terminate rather than compound—error is threat. It is evidence against the self. It is to be avoided, explained away, attributed outward. The thin person under pressure calculates defensively. The deep person under pressure reaches.

This is the fork within the fork. The technological acceleration will separate those who can use the tools from those who cannot. But within those who can use the tools, it will run a second separation: between those who use the tools shallowly—who extract from them a faster version of the same satiated thinness—and those who use them with the urgency and orientation of good eros, drawing from them deeper and deeper approximations of truth, beauty, and creation. The first group will accelerate and plateau. The second will compound. The gap between them, given enough time and enough acceleration, will be the gap between the literate and the illiterate—only faster, and deeper, and harder to close.

Technology, as we have observed, is structurally blind to depth. It optimizes for what is measurable. It cannot generate good eros. It can only amplify whatever eros is already present. Applied to thin desire, it produces thinner desire at scale—more efficient pathways to satiation, more perfect removal of the friction that would otherwise have forced growth. Applied to good eros, it produces something closer to what the tribal life had, which the captivity narratives recorded with bewilderment: the experience of being genuinely claimed—of inhabiting a life where the full self is required, and the full self finds that requirement not oppressive but enlivening.

The fork is coming. The question is not whether to participate in the acceleration—we have established that withdrawal is not the answer, and that the civilizational infrastructure sustaining billions depends on the continuation of progress. The question is whether a community can enter the acceleration carrying the depth that makes compounding possible, and whether that depth can be sustained and transmitted across generations as the curve steepens.

## The Covenantal Architecture and What It Must Do

The Church, at its best, is not primarily a moral enforcement system. It is a depth-transmission system. It is the mechanism by which good eros is evoked, sustained, and passed on—by which the human beings who belong to it are kept in relationship with the attractors (God, covenant, one another, the creation) that pull them outward and upward rather than inward and down.

But the captivity narratives implicate us. What Benjamin Franklin and Crèvecoeur recorded was not merely that tribal communities had something colonial society lacked. It was that formal Christianity—the Christianity of churches, doctrines, sermons, and sacraments—had apparently failed to produce that depth at the civilizational scale of colonial European society. Thousands of people, exposed to both, chose one direction. The depth transmission had failed. The covenantal architecture was structurally in place but the eros it was supposed to carry had gone thin.

The question this poses is not whether Christianity is true. It is whether the institutional forms through which it has been transmitted have been adequate to the task—and whether they can be made adequate to the new task, which is steeper, faster, and operating in an environment designed by powerful economic forces to produce thinness.

Here the specific genius of the Restoration's covenantal theology becomes visible. The covenants of the temple are not merely doctrinal propositions assented to. They are structures of obligation—reciprocal, embodied, relational, and constitutively collective. The individual enters them not as a lone person making a private commitment but as a person being inserted into a web: to God, to spouse, to family, to community, to the dead, to the not-yet-born. This web has the same properties as the tribal web that Crèvecoeur could not quite name: a total claim, a complete interpellation, a web of belonging and obligation and shared meaning dense enough that individual comfort becomes beside the point—not because comfort is forbidden but because the web offers something so much larger than comfort that comfort loses its magnetic pull.

The LDS covenant structure, properly inhabited, is architecturally convex. The Atonement removes the catastrophic downside that makes caution the only rational strategy. Grace blocks the absorbing barriers so that repentance is possible, so that the ordinary distribution of human error and human learning can play out without terminating in spiritual death. Within that bounded downside, the covenantal web creates the dense relational texture—the complete mutual obligation, the shared vision, the common project across generations—that makes the compounding of good eros not merely possible but probable. This is Lehi's argument made structural: the combination of grace (which blocks the absorbing barrier) and covenant (which creates the relational density) produces the conditions under which souls compound into depth rather than thin into satiation.

But the architecture can be inhabited shallowly as well as deeply. A covenant undertaken as an act of compliance—to stay in good standing, to meet expectations, to satisfy the requirements of a system—is architecturally in place but erotically inert. It will not transmit depth. It will not produce the uncanny pull that made colonial captives weep at separation. It will produce, instead, a thin facsimile of belonging—the formal structures of community without the substance that makes community worth belonging to. The same covenant, inhabited with good eros—inhabited by a soul actually provoked by God, actually drawn outward by the weight and beauty of what the covenant describes—becomes something else entirely. It becomes the web. It becomes the thing the captives found and could not explain. It becomes the depth-transmission system the acceleration demands.

## What Restoration Looks Like

The Native Americans had something we destroyed. This is not a sentimental claim. It is an observation about depth, and about the particular form of depth that Patitsas identifies as good eros—the orientation toward beauty and truth and belonging that compounds rather than terminates, that enlivens rather than depletes. The tribal web produced this eros not by doctrine but by structure: by the density of mutual obligation, the completeness of the claim, the rootedness in land and story and ancestors that gave every person a location in something larger than themselves.

We destroyed it. Not always intentionally, and not without also bringing things of genuine value. But we destroyed the structures through which that depth was transmitted, and then we were surprised when the people who had inhabited those structures preferred to return to them rather than remain in our churches. The indictment stands.

What the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is positioned to do—uniquely positioned, by the specific architecture of its covenantal structure—is not to return to tribal life but to restore what tribal life carried. Not to recreate the forms but to recover the substance: the depth, the density, the total claim, the eros that does not terminate. The specific mechanisms the Restoration provides—living prophets who speak to present conditions rather than merely conserving past ones, temple covenants that constitute a web of obligation across time and death, a theology of eternal families that gives the relational web cosmic rather than merely social weight, a doctrine of continuing creation that makes every member a participant in something that is still being built—these are architecturally suited to carrying the depth that tribal life carried, at the scale that civilization requires, and into the acceleration that is coming.

This is a serious claim. It is also a serious indictment of what the Church frequently becomes in practice: a rule-compliance system, a social organization, a mechanism for producing respectable citizens rather than deep ones. The gap between what the architecture can carry and what it typically does carry is the gap between what Crèvecoeur witnessed and what formal Christianity was producing in colonial North America. The gap is not inevitable. It is the gap between covenant inhabited with good eros and covenant inhabited without it. It is the gap between the form and the substance. And it is the gap that the acceleration will, over the next generation or two, make impossible to ignore—because the communities that can produce good eros will compound, and the communities that cannot will plateau, and the difference will become visible in ways that no amount of institutional effort can obscure.

The question, then, is not whether the acceleration is coming. It is whether the Church will be, when it arrives, a depth-transmission system worth belonging to—or whether it will be a formal structure that people leave the moment they find something that actually claims them.

A Zion community is not merely a righteous one. It is a deep one. It is one where the web is dense enough, the eros real enough, the covenant inhabited fully enough, that the people in it could not be persuaded to leave—not because they are prevented from leaving but because what they have is genuinely more compelling than anything outside it. That is what the captives had found. That is what we destroyed. That is what the Restoration is architecturally equipped to restore.

The fork is coming. Which side it finds us on depends on whether we can inhabit the architecture we have been given with the depth it was built to carry.
