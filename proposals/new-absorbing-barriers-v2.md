### The New Absorbing Barriers

You have felt it before. Something grabs you — a mathematical idea, a piece of music, a question about how something in the world actually works, a person who surprises you — and for a moment the usual current of your life reverses. Instead of drifting toward comfort and settled habit, you are pulled. The thing opens rather than closes. You lean toward it and there is more to lean toward. An hour passes and you have not checked your phone. Three hours pass. You are hungry and you do not care yet.

You are in the presence of something that does not discharge.

Most desires have the structure of hunger: they arise, they demand attention, they are satisfied, and the world returns to baseline or slightly below it. The satisfaction of eating does not make food more interesting — it ends the interest. The satisfaction of a solved problem, a completed project, a resolved argument, an arrived destination: in each case, what was beckoning goes quiet once you obtain it. The trajectory bends downward toward equilibrium. This is not a pathology; it is the ordinary structure of most human wanting.

But not all desire works this way. There is another kind — rarer, more powerful, recognizable by its opposite structure. The more you pursue it, the more there is to pursue. You make progress into it and the horizon of the possible expands rather than contracts. The person deep in genuine mathematics does not find the subject smaller for having learned more of it. They find it larger. The craftsman who has spent thirty years on a discipline does not experience their relationship to the craft as one of diminishing returns — the opposite is nearer the truth. They see more than the novice sees, not less. What they understand they understand more deeply, and what they do not understand appears, in proportion, more interesting than threatening. This is the structure of desire that does not discharge. It compounds. The Orthodox theologian Timothy Patitsas, drawing from Plato and the great Christian tradition he inherits, calls it good eros.

Eros in the ancient sense was never primarily sexual. It was the soul's orientation toward the beautiful — that particular kind of longing that reaches toward what is genuinely, inexhaustibly worth wanting. What Plato saw, and what the Christian tradition preserved and deepened, was that this orientation is not merely pleasant — it is the organizing principle of a life. The soul that has found its eros is not merely happier than the soul that hasn't. It is pointed in a direction from which righteous action, creative building, and genuine love emerge as natural consequences rather than costly impositions. The eros does the work that discipline merely approximates. And unlike discipline, which depletes, eros accumulates.

The problem is that good eros is not the only thing that feels like eros.

Technology, particularly in its modern algorithmic form, is extraordinarily good at producing experiences that carry the surface phenomenology of eros — the urgency, the pull, the sense that something worth wanting is just ahead — while systematically blocking its actual structure. The scroll does not compound. The click does not open. The dopamine of the arriving notification is indistinguishable, in the moment, from the dopamine of a genuine discovery — but one of them leaves the world a little smaller afterward and the other leaves it larger. The tragedy is that the counterfeit operates more efficiently than the genuine article. It delivers its surface sensation with less friction, less uncertainty, less demand on the person. And this is precisely why it crowds the genuine out.

Technology relies on engineering. Engineering relies on logic. And logic can only optimize for what is measurable. The measurable satisfactions — clicks, calories, time-on-app, purchases, frictionless transactions — are the low-order ones, the ones that yield to iteration and A/B testing. Depth does not yield to measurement. Wonder does not yield to measurement. The quality of a life's interior does not yield to measurement. And so the systems we build, optimizing relentlessly for what they can see, become structurally blind to what they cannot — and in displacing friction they also displace the difficulty that was the only pathway to the depth the friction was guarding. Every app that removes a small difficulty without understanding why the difficulty existed has moved a wall. Every algorithm that delivers pleasure more efficiently has made the genuine provocation slightly harder to find. The walls are not obvious. They look like comfort. They feel like having arrived. And critically: they do not merely prevent you from going deeper. They persuade you that there is no deeper to go.

This persuasion is the new absorbing barrier.

What makes it particularly insidious is what it does across generations. The thinning is not catastrophic in any given moment. No one wakes up and decides to stop wanting deep things. It happens gradually, in the way that Hemingway described going bankrupt: first slowly, then all at once. My family dinners are less frequent and less dense than those I grew up with; my father's were less frequent and less dense than his father's. The stories that were told, the arguments that unfolded, the texture of knowing one another at close range over time — these things thin without announcement. The thinning feels like efficiency, like appropriate adaptation to a busier world, until the moment, if it comes, when you encounter someone or something that has not thinned and you experience the contrast as a kind of vertigo. Something you had forgotten the name of suddenly has a name again.

A puzzling pattern emerged along the American frontier that troubled the finest minds of the eighteenth century. Thousands of white settlers — men, women, and children — were taken captive by Native American tribes. This was common enough that a whole literary genre, the captivity narrative, arose around it. But the disquieting part was what happened when these captives were offered their freedom. They refused to come home. Not some of them — a striking and well-documented majority. Benjamin Franklin noted the phenomenon directly in his 1753 letter to Peter Collinson: "When an Indian child has been brought up among us, taught our language and habituated to our customs, yet if he goes to see his relations and makes one Indian ramble with them, there is no persuading him ever to return." J. Hector St. John de Crèvecoeur, writing in *Letters from an American Farmer*, found it no less baffling: "There must be in their social bond something singularly captivating, and far superior to anything to be boasted of among us." Colonial authorities organized formal return expeditions. Captives were physically restrained. They wept at separation. Meanwhile, Native Americans taken into colonial households would seize the first opportunity to slip back into the forest. The migration ran almost entirely in one direction.

What did tribal life have that colonial society did not? Not comfort. Not security. Not material advantage of any kind. What it had was good eros at the civilizational scale — a complete claim on the person, a web of obligation and belonging so total that individual comfort became beside the point. You were needed in a way that you felt continuously. You knew exactly who you would grieve if they died, and who would grieve you. The eros of belonging and purpose and mutual visibility did not discharge when satisfied. It could not be satisfied. It was the water in which you swam and its presence was not felt as satisfaction but as orientation — the kind of orientation whose absence, once you had known it, was felt as something close to suffocation.

Here is the indictment that Franklin and Crèvecoeur were honest enough to register but not quite able to absorb: several centuries of Christianity had, at the civilizational scale of colonial European society, apparently failed to transmit that depth broadly or universally. This is not an argument against Christianity. It is an observation about the gap between what a tradition can carry at its best and what it typically delivers at its scale — and about how a civilization organized around material accumulation and rational self-interest can go formally Christian while quietly evacuating the interior life that Christianity is supposed to cultivate. The captivity phenomenon did not merely indict colonial manners. It indicted something structural.

The answer cannot be to reverse the process. We cannot return to tribal life — not because it was inferior, but because it is simply impossible at modern scale. Eight billion people are alive, the vast majority of them only because of the agricultural, medical, and logistical infrastructure that industrial civilization built. The grain supply. Vaccines. Surgery. Water treatment. These systems do not scale down. They require the civilization that produced them, which requires the division of labor and the commerce and the complexity, all of it. We depend on progress not merely as an aspiration but as a physical precondition for the survival of billions of people now in existence. And beyond necessity: progress is a fundamental drive of mankind. The desire to know more, build more, go further — that is not a pathology to be managed. It is part of what it means to be the kind of beings who can leave the Garden.

The question is whether you can pursue progress without thinning humanity in the process. Whether you can automate things without automating people. Whether you can commodify what should be commodified — materials, logistics, routine processes — without commodifying the human relationships and human making that give life its texture.

This question has always been live. It is more urgent now.

---

AI is not a faster printing press. It is leverage applied to cognition itself — to the very capacity that previously set human beings apart from their tools. The printing press created one fork: those who could read versus those who could not. AI threatens to create a recursive fork, because it amplifies thinking, and those who use it well think faster and better, which makes them more capable of using it even better, which makes them faster still. The gap does not grow linearly. It compounds. And as it compounds far enough, the people on the accelerating side begin to inhabit a different cadence of discovery, accumulation, and possibility than those they leave behind.

But the more important fork is not the one between those who use AI and those who don't. It is the fork within those who use it.

Applied to thin desire — to the already-counterfeit eros of ordinary digital life, where desire is constantly stimulated and constantly discharged — AI produces a more efficient version of the same thinness. Faster satiation. More precisely targeted comfort. A better optimized path to the same plateau. Applied to good eros — to a soul that is genuinely seized by something, genuinely provoked toward depth — AI produces something closer to a multiplier of what the tribal life had. Not comfort, but capability. Not efficiency in the service of satiation, but leverage in the service of genuine creation.

Technology can only amplify what is already there. Applied to good eros, it compounds. Applied to counterfeit eros, it plateaus faster.

This is why the question of depth is not merely spiritual or personal. It is civilizational and competitive. Communities and individuals who enter the acceleration carrying genuine good eros — who are actually provoked by what they are building, actually drawn by the inexhaustible beauty of what they are reaching toward — will compound. Those who enter it carrying the counterfeit will accelerate briefly and then plateau, and the gap between them will, given enough time and enough leverage, look evolutionary. Not metaphorically. History's great human forks have followed exactly this logic. The gap between the literate and the illiterate after the printing press was not merely economic. It restructured what kinds of lives were imaginable to each group — what could be wanted, what could be built, what could be transmitted. A similar restructuring is coming, and whether it finds a given community on the compounding or the plateauing side will depend less on which tools they have access to than on whether the eros they carry into those tools is genuine.

---

This is what Lehi is trying to tell his sons. Second Nephi chapter two is not a philosophical treatise. It is a guide to this exact problem: how moral agents, living inside history and subject to all its volatility and all its new absorbing barriers, can ratchet toward genuine depth rather than toward the efficient thinness that always looks like comfort and always feels like arrival.

The Atonement does not primarily remove punishment. It removes the absorbing barriers that prevent good eros from functioning. The concavity that makes every failure terminal — the spiritual death that waits at the bottom of every moral stumble — is what traps a person in defensive calculation, in the Adam-in-the-Garden strategy of staying within the known framework and making no bets the rulebook doesn't authorize. With that barrier lifted, variance becomes productive again. The person can afford to reach. Can afford to risk. Can afford to commit to something larger than themselves without the commitment becoming a catastrophic exposure. Righteousness ceases to be a cost and becomes what it always was meant to be: an eros. The natural direction of a soul that has been shown what is actually worth wanting, and has been freed from the barriers that prevented it from wanting that way.

The counterfeit eros of our technological moment trains us to expect that the answer to desire is the discharge of desire. That the point of wanting is having. That the correct response to being provoked is the elimination of the provocation. Good eros runs the other direction entirely. It says that the provocation is the gift, that the depth is inexhaustible, that the soul following it faithfully will find itself capable of things it could not have imagined from where it started.

The recovery is not nostalgia and it is not retreat. It is not the replication of tribal forms in a world those forms cannot sustain. It is the restoration of what those forms were carrying — the recovered ability to want the right things, to feel the provocation toward depth rather than satiation, and to find the depth, at last, responsive. Where there is no vision the people perish. This was always true. In the acceleration, the speed of the forgetting is what makes it dangerous. The absorbing barriers of the modern world do not merely trap us in thinness. They erase our memory that anything beyond them exists — until the flatness feels like nature rather than deprivation, and we mistake our plateau for a summit.

The Atonement removes the barrier. Good eros provides the direction. And the covenantal architecture of the Restoration, properly inhabited, is the structure designed to transmit that direction across generations — to be, in the acceleration as in every prior age, the thing that the captives had found and could not explain and wept to leave.
