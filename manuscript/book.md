These phenomena—the abrupt crystallization of sodium acetate, the segregation of nuts, the sprouting of a lively party from a dull one—are manifestations of symmetry breaking, a profound concept that has arisen in physics over the last couple of centuries. Symmetry breaking occurs when a system's underlying laws exhibit invariance, but the actual state the system settles into violates that symmetry. The rules allow for perfect balance, yet reality tips the scales, selecting one configuration from many possibilities. In the sodium acetate solution, the super-saturated state is symmetric in its liquidity, but nucleation breaks translational and rotational symmetry as crystals form in a specific pattern. The Brazil nut effect disrupts the homogeneity of the mixture, breaking the symmetry of particle distribution and separating brazil nuts from the rest. Same with the party.

Perhaps the most common concept of symmetry is horizontal symmetry in a single dimension like what a human face exhibits. The left side mirrors the right side. There are higher levels of symmetry too. For example, a four pointed snow flake exhibits symmetry across two directions. The top mirrors the bottom and the left mirrors the right. More generally, symmetry is the idea of something remaining the same under transformation, and the more transformations you can make to something without changing its state or appearance, the more symmetry it exhibits. There are all different kinds of transformations. A six pointed snowflake exhibits higher rotational symmetry for example than a four pointed snow flake. You can rotate a six pointed snowflake to six different positions without changing its appearance, while a four pointed snowflake can only be rotated to four different positions. A perfect circle can be rotated to an infinite number of positions without changing its appearance and thus exhibits infinite two-dimensional rotational symmetry.

Symmetry breaking is the idea that even though the underlying rules contain no bias or preference, in reality the objects governed by those rules somehow choose a direction, orientation, or state that reflects bias and direction. In fact, it was this very conundrum that set Ludwig Boltzmann to work on the problem that eventually gave rise to the concept of symmetry breaking. Specifically, given the symmetry of Newton’s laws of physics, Boltzmann wanted to understand what rules at the atomic level account for fast moving molecules (hot) to move towards and mix with slow moving molecules (cold). What laws of physics caused temperatures to mix and move towards equilibrium (in this case, increasing the symmetry of a body of gas).

Per modern science the universe began in the Big Bang as a hot, symmetric soup where everything was unified. As it cooled and expanded, symmetries broke in stages giving rise to differentiation and existence. Matter, physical forces, even time emitted from these stages of symmetry breaking. Here's a rundown of the key ones:

- Grand Unified Symmetry Breaking (around 10^-35 seconds after the Big Bang): Early on, all fundamental forces (gravity, strong nuclear, weak nuclear, and electromagnetic) might have been one super-force in some theories (Grand Unified Theories or GUTs). This symmetry broke first, splitting off the strong force (which holds atomic nuclei together) from the electroweak force. This created the distinction needed for quarks to form protons and neutrons—basically, the building blocks of atoms.
- Electroweak Symmetry Breaking (around 10^-12 seconds): The electroweak force then broke into the electromagnetic force (handling light, electricity, and magnetism) and the weak force (responsible for radioactive decay). This is where the famous Higgs mechanism kicks in—the Higgs field "freezes" and breaks the symmetry, giving particles mass. Without it, everything would zip around at light speed, no solid matter.
- Chiral Symmetry Breaking (around 10^-6 seconds): In quantum chromodynamics (QCD), the strong force's symmetry between left- and right-handed particles breaks, giving quarks mass and allowing protons and neutrons to have the properties we see. This paves the way for complex nuclei and chemistry.
- Other Breaks in the Mix: There's also the breaking of matter-antimatter symmetry (CP violation), which explains why there's more matter than antimatter, letting the universe exist instead of annihilating itself. And in cosmology, during inflation (a rapid expansion phase), scalar field symmetries break to end the inflation and seed galaxy formation.
Each break isn't only destruction; it's creation of space for new possibilities. The separation of forces lets them behave differently and interact with each other: electromagnetism powers stars and gadgets, while the weak force enables fusion in the sun. Electricity separating from magnetism—originally unified in electromagnetism--enabled them to interact, giving rise to the possibility of motors and generators, turning one into the other. Without these distinctions, no engines, no chemistry, no life.

Probability is another, yet unfinished, domain of human knowledge with a long but dramatic rise in cultural comprehension and with yet compounding contributions to practical philosophy. The Greeks, otherwise paragons of mathematical progress, largely ignored probability as did the Arabs and Hindus. Prior to Blaise Pascal in the 17th century the best history of probabilistic thinking is found in the various legal systems of the world. It was in the courts that we were forced to most directly confront the technical aspects of conviction. What kind of evidence can we consider adequate to do away with uncertainty sufficient enough to condemn a man to death? As one illustration of how much our thinking in this domain has changed consider that it was commonly believed in Europe and in China that confession induced by torture was ample evidence of guilt or that annuities in England were sold at the same price to all people regardless of age. Pascal was the first to introduce a mathematical theory of probability and even after Pascal and Fermat had begun to formalize the discipline and others had further developed it, it was embraced more by gamblers than by scientists for two hundred years.

In 1860 29-year-old James Clerk Maxwell would finally bridge the gap between probabilistic theory and scientific application. A few had made gestures ahead of him, noting that natural phenomena indeed conformed to known statistical distributions. The most popular scientific use of statistical distributions at the time was analyzing errors in astronomical predictions - a method Maxwell was very familiar with. Predicting the position of heavenly bodies was tricky business since even when you had the math right, you had to deal with errors in your observations - both the observations upon which the prediction would be formulated and also errors in the observations of the predicted result. Over time it was observed that the noise in these observations and predictions, the size of each error, followed a normal distribution. Before realizing this, it could be very difficult to confirm or disconfirm competing theories if both theories made predictions within the range of observational error. But with enough observations one could show that in fact, the confirming observations did or did not vary from the prediction according to the expected statistical distribution. It was by this kind of analysis that the normal distribution was discovered and formalized by Carl Friederich Gauss.

Beyond this it had also begun to be noticed that other phenomena conformed to predictable distributions. One example that gives some insight into how probability was understood at the time is that when Adolphe Quetelet began showing that social phenomena like crime rates and suicide rates had different statistical distributions of likelihood for men than for women it was argued that such observations conflicted with individual free agency.

Ironically, Quetelet’s most popular book subtitled Essays on Social Physics predated the landmark applications of statistical analysis to actual physics. All of this development, although foundational to Maxwell’s breakthrough, fell far short in itself of breaking through one of the most entrenched and defining ideas of science which is that science was about reducing phenomena to the smallest components possible so as to make as precise predictions as possible.

In the case of using statistical analysis to confirm or reject predictions about the positions of heavenly bodies, the uncertainty stemmed only from the noise of imperfect observation, not from any uncertainty about the values of predictions made by gravitational theory. Statistical methods helped to manage the noise of observation in order to confirm the precision of theories and laws. Maxwell would turn this method on its head, making statistical distributions themselves the object of his predictions.

At stake was the case for kinetic theory - the theory that gasses were constituted of atoms and that their properties could be predicted by applying Newton’s laws of motion at the molecular level. The existence of atoms would continue to be disputed for decades and this battle would underlie the development of thermodynamics until the end of the century.

One of the challenges to the kinetic theory was that if gas molecules were moving fast enough to exert the observed pressure on the walls of their container, then it is confusing why it takes so long for the scent of a released gas to make its way across the room. It seemed this process should be nearly instantaneous if gas molecules were moving so fast. Ernst Mach had speculated, correctly it turned out, that perhaps the reason for slow propagation was that the molecules were banging into each other more regularly than expected which would slow their progression across a room. But Mach's efforts at modeling this idea were frustrated by his simple and incorrect assumption that all molecules in the gas of a given temperature move at the same speed.

Maxwell set out to more thoroughly describe the variation of velocities gas molecules might exhibit. The entire proof is surprisingly short and accessible even though it does require a bit of math. In a nutshell, Maxwell showed that if you have a box of gas particles, and assume the three component speeds of each particle (speeds along each of the three axis of three dimensional space) are randomly assigned (along a uniform distribution - i.e. no velocity more likely than any other), then the speeds of those particles (speed being the absolute speed through space implied by the three directional velocities) will follow a distribution closely related to the normal distribution for which he derived the precise formula. This can be easily and intuitively exhibited using an excel spreadsheet. (may include an example in appendix)

Let us summarize. Without any experimental observation beyond what was generally available, Maxwell reasoned that if component velocities of molecules were independent and uniformly distributed, then total velocity would follow what is known today as the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution.

But why call it the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution? We've heard nothing of Boltzmann.

Maxwell received much recognition for his paper and offered strong resolutions to outstanding challenges to atomic theory but one man more than all the rest would see that the true potential for Maxwell’s ideas went far deeper than merely stating the statistical distribution of molecule velocities in a gas. Ludwig Boltzmann was enthralled with Maxwell’s paper even once waxing poetic about its elegance, “First the variations in velocity develop majestically, then the equations of state enter on one side, the equations of motion on the other; ever higher surges the chaos of formulas…".

For the next ten years Boltzmann would think about and add onto Maxwell’s work. His enthrallment was with an idea sparked decades prior but only recently given a name and at that point still not formalized theoretically at the atomic level called entropy. Entropy is famously abstract, once described by John Von Neumann as a subject nobody understood.

The roots of the idea of entropy began in 1824 from an observation made by the French engineer Sadi Carnot in his book Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire - such a lovely title I had to include it. Sadi’s father was a prolific man himself in science, war and politics. His contribution most relevant to us here is that he was the first to prove the impossibility of perpetual motion.

Combustion and steam engines had been around for a while, but no theory existed to guide the effort to improve their efficiency (a fine example of the fact that it is tinkerers rather than theoreticians who generally make the technological breakthroughs and the theoreticians who explain the technology after the fact which can be helpful for improving inventions once invented - even more interestingly Carnot himself points this fact out in his book). Carnot described the key components of a combustion engine, outlined the combustion cycle according to its key phases, and was even able to define upper limits on the power an engine could generate which enabled measuring engine efficiency. One of Carnot’s key observations was that it was the difference between the temperature of the engine’s chambers that defined the maximum limit of the power an engine was capable of producing.

Carnot having abstracted the heat engine into its key components, Rudolf Clausias worked within Carnot’s model to further categorize the thermal byproducts of a running engine including the effects of heat leaving the system. It was Clausias who gave entropy its name which he chose in part for its similarity in sound and close physical relation to energy. Specifically, the more entropy a system has, the less potential energy it has, or the less capacity it has for accomplishing work. (link to separation here??)

Remember Carnot’s observation that it is the difference in temperatures between the hot and cold chambers of the engine that determine the amount of work the engine can perform. Clausius figured ways to quantify this disparity and in 1866 gave it the name of entropy.

To illustrate this idea more plainly examine the illustration below of a Stirling Engine. At the base of the engine is a chamber filled with gas, separated into two halves by a movable divider. The engine begins with the divider half way up, dividing the chamber into two equal halves. Now imagine placing the engine on top of a block of ice. The cold will be conducted through the wall of the container cooling the gas in the bottom half of the container. As we know from having examined the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution, as a gas cools the average velocity of the molecules also reduces which in turn reduces the pressure. As the pressure in the bottom half of the container is reduced relative to the top half, the divider is pressed down to the bottom of the container. As the divider is lowered it reduces the pressure in the top half and raises the pressure in the bottom half reversing the temperature disparity. The pressure and thus temperature of the bottom half of the container is raised until the point where the bottom half is more highly pressurized than the top half and begins pushing the divider up until the disparity is reversed again and reverses the process again. As long as one side of the chamber is either heated or cooled sufficiently using an external source of heat or cold, this process will continue. As the divider is moved repeatedly up and down, a mechanical assembly transitions this back and forth energy into the circular motion of the flywheel.

Think back to Carnot and his proposition that it is the difference in temperature of the two sub-chambers that matters. This means that to speed up the engine you not only can more aggressively cool the cold side, but you could also heat up the hot side. If you are running the Stirling Engine at room temperature, with a block of ice on the bottom, you could increase the speed simply by resting your hand on the top of the engine’s base. Or if you are heating the bottom side instead of cooling it, you could place cubes of ice on the top half to increase the velocity of the flywheel. There are excellent demonstrations of Stirling Engines online if you’d like to see this effect demonstrated.

Now consider what will happen if the ice is removed from the bottom of the engine. The chamber divider will continue moving until pressure is equalized between the top and the bottom sub-chambers. Without either a hot or cold source continuing to perpetrate an imbalance between the two sides the divider will cease oscillating. This state of imbalance is entropy. The higher the imbalance the lower the entropy. The lower the entropy the higher capacity for work there is in the system.

In a closed system the 2nd law of thermodynamics states that entropy can only increase or stay the same. Once maximum entropy is reached there is no more work that can be performed by the system. All of this understanding had been developing since 1824 and culminated in 1866 when Rudolf Clausius encapsulated the idea in a word - entropy.

But what was not established was a theory at the molecular level to justify the 2nd law of thermodynamics. If atomic theory was correct and gasses were made up of molecules bouncing all around, what was the explanation at the molecular level, justified by Newtonian mechanics, for why entropy always increases? In terms of an engine, what is the explanation for why the difference between hot and cold always reduces? It was to this problem that Boltzmann now dedicated himself.

The path Boltzmann ended up traversing will be for the purpose of this book equally important to Boltzmann’s final conclusion. Boltzmann published two major attempts at solving the problem, the second as an answer for challenges to the first. But the second attempt was nothing like a revision of the first - it was completely different, and it was as creative as Maxwell’s introduction of statistics to physics in the first place.

In his first attempt Boltzmann went at the problem directly. He stated assumptions about how molecules collide and then showed how as a direct result the same equilibrium illustrated by Maxwell - the same distribution of particle velocities - was the result. But even though he corroborated Maxwell’s findings, Maxwell himself was unimpressed. As a counterpoint Maxwell put forward a thought experiment. He imagined a container divided into two chambers. Along the wall dividing the chambers there was a door, operated by a demon, who, observing the gas molecules bouncing around, could at his discretion open and close the door as molecules happened to approach it, letting some molecules through and not others. If the demon were to implement a policy of allowing fast molecules through but blocking slow molecules, then the temperature disparity between the two chambers would, rather than reach parity, move away from each other as the side which was gaining fast molecules increased in temperature while the side losing fast molecules lowered in temperature.

Of course, there is no demon so why should Boltzmann be required to answer such a bizarre challenge? Maxwell used the demon to illustrate the idea that it is possible, even though extremely unlikely, that such a scenario could unfold on its own. Somewhere, probably tied up in all the assumptions Boltzmann had made about particle interactions, there was a mistake. To make matters worse, Maxwell’s challenge was followed up by another, this time from Boltzmann’s close friend and fellow countryman Joseph Loschmidt. Loschmidt’s argument was more straightforward, stemming directly and cleanly from Newton’s laws. In Newtonian mechanics there is no time asymmetry. What this means is that any system of motion described by Newtonian mechanics could run backwards if all the forces were reversed without violating Newton’s laws. Another way to put this would be to say that if you observed a system in motion you could not prove which way time was flowing merely by observing the motion of the system, or said a third way, running the video backwards and forwards, you would not be able to prove which is forward and which is backward. Within the context of familiar examples you may well be able to tell that the canon ball is moving away from, rather than towards the canon, but Newton couldn’t help you prove it.

Although this isn’t a big practical problem for identifying the direction of canon balls, when the very question you are trying to answer is why a thermodynamic system moves towards equilibrium rather than away from it by using Newton’s laws, it becomes a very big problem. Loschmidt pointed out that for every sequence of interactions that moves a system towards equilibrium, if the forces were simply reversed on every molecule, one would observe a perfectly legitimate example of a system moving away from equilibrium. This is the same problem Maxwell highlighted derived a different way. It had long been known that this is never observed in practice - but why?

This double challenge from Maxwell and Loschmidt was enough to send Boltzmann back to the drawing board and it was these challenges that pressed him to formulate one of the most foundational scientific ideas of the modern world.

If Newton’s laws were inadequate to prove why entropy always increases or why systems relentlessly moved towards equilibrium Boltzmann would have to radically change his approach. Newton’s laws were the absolute foundation of physics - how could one even imagine solving the problem without invoking them or at the very least being consistent with them?

One thing Boltzmann did have however was an intuition that although Maxwell and Loschmidt were right, the likelihood of the scenarios they described was extremely low. Seeing a system move away from equilibrium would “require a conspiracy among the atoms” as he put it. If that were true, perhaps a good place to start would be to try to quantify precisely how likely such a conspiracy would be.

This time instead of making meticulous assumptions about Newtonian molecular interaction, Boltzmann ignored Newtonian rules and made almost no assumptions besides that the interactions of the molecules was so complicated as to be for the practical purposes of the problem at hand essentially random.

Freed of the constraint of having to demonstrate that Newton’s rules were the causal force of systems tending towards equilibrium, diffusion, and maximum entropy it dawned on Boltzmann that systems achieved equilibrium simply because in all of the random jostling it turns out there are exponentially more ways molecules can be arranged so as to be evenly distributed than there are ways wherein they can be, say, on the left half of a container.

Let’s move this discussion into a more familiar metaphor by going back to talking about a jar of nuts. Suppose you fill a jar with two kinds of peanuts. First you pour in unsalted peanuts and on top of those you pour in salted peanuts in equal measure leaving space at the top of the can. Now imagine you shake the can vigorously for several minutes. When you examine the contents after shaking them you will expect the salted peanuts and the unsalted peanuts to be more or less mixed up even though they began separated from each other. Boltzmann realized that trying to explain entropy from Newtonian mechanics was a lot like trying to explain why the two kinds of peanuts were more or less evenly mixed after shaking the can.

There is no physical law which demands they be mixed. It is simply more likely, overwhelmingly more likely, that they will be. Suppose we think about all of the different ways the peanuts can be arranged inside the can. Each one of those arrangements we’ll identify as a distinct microstate. A particular microstate defines the precise placement of every peanut in the can. Now say we define the idea of a macrostate as the percent of salted peanuts, which began on the top, that rest in the bottom half of the can. Before we shake we can describe our macrostate as 0% because none of the salted peanuts are in the bottom half. After vigorous shaking we would expect that 0% to change to somewhere near 50%. The reason this expectation is warranted is not because the laws of physics demand it, but only because of all the different microstates the peanuts can achieve, almost all of them relatively speaking correspond to an approximately 50% of salted peanuts being in the bottom half.

If we wanted to compute the likelihood that after shaking the can all of the salted peanuts would end up on the bottom half (corresponding to a macrostate of 100%) the formula would look like this:

Probability of 100% Macrostate (all salted peanuts on the bottom) = (# of microstates that correspond to 100% macrostate)/(total # of possible microstates)

We can even do the math here for a discrete two dimensional example. In the picture below you can see we have a can that holds twenty layers of peanuts, each layer ten peanuts wide for a total of 200 peanuts. The total number of microstates, or total number of ways 200 peanuts can be arranged in this can, is then 200! (or factorial of 200). That’s a really big number. The next question is how many of those 200! microstates are such that every salted peanut is on the bottom half. Well there are 100! ways of arranging 100 salted peanuts on the bottom half of the can. But for each one of those arrangements, we can also rearrange the unsalted peanuts on the top of the can for which there are the same number of possibilities. Therefore the number of total microstates that correspond to all salted peanuts on the bottom half of the can is 100! X 100! which is also a very big number. Taking the ratio we get:

(100! X 100!) / 200! = 1/90548514656103281165404177077484163874504589675413336841320

In other words, you have a 1 in 90,548,514,656,103,281,165,404,177,077,484,163,874,504,589,675,413,336,841,320 chance of having all the salted peanuts on the bottom half of the can after vigorous extended shaking! That is worse odds, unfathomably worse, than picking the right grain of sand from all the grains of sand on earth. Conversely, by the same math we can compute that the chances of having exactly one half of each color in the bottom half (and thereby the top half too) is 11.24%!

The reason entropy always increases is simply that of all the possible arrangements a system can embody, the overwhelming portion of them correspond to instances of or near maximum entropy.

With this insight Boltzmann was able to capture the idea of entropy in an equation which today graces his tombstone: S = k Log W where S is entropy, k is a constant and W is the total number of possible microstates corresponding to the macrostate for which entropy is being quantified.

Now, let’s complicate the peanut example a bit. Instead of salted and unsalted peanuts let us consider peanuts and brazil nuts (both without shells) and instead of beginning with them separated and grouped together, let’s begin with them evenly mixed throughout the can. What will happen now when we shake the can? According to our analysis so far, we would expect them to remain evenly mixed - there are, after all, many more arrangements that exhibit even mixing than arrangements where the brazil nuts are separated from the peanuts - but when we shake the can we will see the brazil nuts rising to the top and the peanuts sinking to the bottom. This phenomenon even has a name - the brazil nut effect. How do we square this with the Second Law of Thermodynamics which insists entropy must always increase? How is this emergent separation, emergent order, accounted for?

It turns out the Brazil Nut effect is tremendously complicated to properly model, but we can illustrate some key points by modeling it simplistically and focusing on a single effect. The most obvious difference between peanuts and brazil nuts within the context of our purpose is that brazil nuts are bigger than peanuts. Let’s pretend our objective is to get brazil nuts to the top of the can, and within that context we’ll evaluate whether shaking the can is a good strategy to achieve that. Well, with each sufficiently vigorous shake the nuts are going to be suspended in the air and come crashing down again in a somewhat different arrangement than they were previously in. Now we hope that when the nuts land, some of the peanuts that were previously higher than the brazil nuts or at the same elevation will fall in below the brazil nuts allowing the brazil nuts to rest higher than they began. But with each shake we also take a risk: there's a chance the opposite will happen and that the peanuts will escape above its adjacent brazil nut and come down on top of it, lowering the brazil nut’s position.

It should be noted that both of these possibilities are very real - both types of outcomes do occur if the can is shaken vigorously enough, long enough. But which outcome is more likely? Peanuts falling below brazil nuts or brazil nuts falling below peanuts? Well, for a brazil nut to occupy space previously occupied by a peanut, it will need more space than just one peanut’s worth. It will need several peanuts’ worth of space, which means that several peanuts will need to simultaneously vacate that space at the same time a brazil nut is positioned to assume it. Conversely, when a single brazil nut vacates its space it immediately leaves enough room for several peanuts to enter into. So in the random jostling created by shaking the can, we will expect more opportunities for peanuts to fall below brazil nuts than for brazil nuts to fall below peanuts. The key is that an asymmetry in the system - in this case the difference in size between peanuts and brazil nuts, and the asymmetry of gravity - causes the system to tend in a particular direction. We call this effect a convex effect. In fact, the brazil nut effect is one particular example of the broader phenomenon called granular convection. ENTER SYMMETRY BREAKING: At its heart, convexity is an asymmetry. (Read this next part carefully). The graph below represents a convex relationship between x and y. This is because when x increases, y increases by more than y decreases if x decreases. Or in other words, equal changes in x result in unequal changes in y, depending on which direction x is changed. Below you see the changes in y for an equal increase and decrease in x by amount α. You see that the absolute value of the change in y is bigger when x increases than it is when x decreases. Because the curve is upward this is a convex relationship. A downward curve that steepened as x increases would indicate a concave relationship.

You may be thinking - yeah yeah, it’s called a curve, it’s called a nonlinear relationship, this is pretty basic algebra, why are you going on and on about this? I’m trying to help you perceive the idea of non-linearity in terms of asymmetry and the paradigm of convexity embodies that perspective. The power of convexity is well known in domains like investing where compounded returns exhibit this ever accelerating effect (x is time, y is return) but what is less commonly recognized are the benefits of convexity for dealing with volatility. Suppose x is some recurring event, and y represents some impact that results directly from x and which is more beneficial the higher it is. In such a situation, for any given average, the higher the volatility of x (the more it jumps around), the better off you are, averaging over multiple iterations. Let’s look at an example.

A friend of mine who is a landscaping professional was instructing me recently on how to best help my lawn weather the drought we are presently experiencing. We need to do what we can to get as much benefit as possible for every gallon of water we use. He explained that if I water less frequently, but for longer periods of time the grass roots will chase the water down as it seeps deeper into the earth. Deeper roots make a more resilient lawn. This principle represents a convex response to watering. If we map this example to the pictured graph, y represents positive impact on grass health, and x is length of time watering for a given watering session. Instead of watering the lawn everyday for length x, my friend says we are better off watering the lawn every other day for a length of 2x (or x + x) and every other day skipping watering altogether (x-x). We have not increased the total amount of water we expend, but we achieve a bigger positive impact on the lawn since the increase in the magnitude of y by doubling x on the days that we water is bigger than the reduction in y on days we don’t water (or reduce x by amount x to zero). We can say that lawn health is convex to the length of watering sessions.

Here we begin to see compounding asymmetry, or how convex positions accumulate once initial symmetry has been broken. The Brazil Nut Effect is a specific case of the more general phenomenon called granular convection. The intuition to take away is that under conditions of convexity, uncertainty takes on a whole new persona. Volatility becomes a (potential) ally rather than being an impediment - specifically, volatility that has an asymmetric effect on some object one cares about. While theoretically possible, it is practically impossible due to all of the variables that would have to be measured and tracked. In fact, this is precisely the change Boltzmann made. His first approach was the big left brain computational one, which failed, and then he switched to convexity.

The key characteristic of convex and concave functions is asymmetry. Y changes more as x moves in one direction than it does when x moves in the other direction. In a scenario with many transactions, like shaking a can hundreds of times, even small asymmetries will compound becoming ultimately an overpowering force with predictable macro effects.

In the case of the nuts, even though it is possible on any given shake for a brazil nut to lose elevation, over time the higher likelihood of it gaining elevation will prevail.

In deriving the equation for entropy, Boltzmann traversed the path from initially trying to precisely compute the micro mechanics applying Newton’s laws, to understanding that he ought to be thinking in terms of predictable effects at a scale higher than individual atoms. What Boltzmann showed was not just that we need not model the system at the lowest level to be able to make useful predictions, he showed that there are no principles operating at that level that will accurately predict universal macro behavior since theoretically there do exist microstates which if achieved could absolutely defy the second law of thermodynamics. In the Brazil Nut example, there do exist initial conditions of nut placement which when combined with a particular sequence of very precise shaking would cause the brazil nuts to go down. But those initial conditions are so few, and the sequence of required shakes so rare, relative to the total domain of possibilities, they simply never materialize. Boltzmann showed that the second law was in fact only probabilistic, and that crucially, the path forward was to quantify this probability as a fundamental law of nature rather than discard the second law. In so doing, he finished the work Maxwell began of using statistical distributions as the currency of prediction rather than only a method of checking simple predictions.

When no asymmetries are present, when molecules are all essentially identical, diffusion is the outcome. When asymmetries are present and make certain small-scale outcomes more likely than others, these asymmetries can compound into macro-level effects like the separation of brazil nuts from peanuts or as we’ll see later in the formation of the cosmos, the creation of the elements, stars, planets, solar systems and galaxies or even the emergence of life and of a life worth living. This is symmetry breaking.

## Chapter 2 — Information Theory

It may be countered that I'm putting too much on Boltzmann's breakthrough, that, revolutionary as it was for thermodynamics, to characterize Boltzmann's equation as the turning point for probabilistic paradigms in science is too much. It may be claimed that building the foundation of convexity upon this foundation is especially unjustified since the mathematics of convexity has a history all its own and one quite independent of Ludwig Boltzmann. To establish the broad generality of Boltzmann's idea and to also give an example of its practical impact we will turn our attention towards information theory.

In thermodynamics, the higher the entropy the less clumping and the more diffusion there is in a container of gas. In information theory, given a message of a particular length, the entropy will be higher the more randomly distributed the symbols that make it up. For example, the string of symbols 1111100000 contains less information and therefore lower entropy (in information theory entropy is equivalent to information) than the string 1001000111 even though both messages are of equal length and contain the same number of 1's and 0's.

Shannon's insight was that the way to think about information is to consider the amount of uncertainty a piece of information resolves (uncertainty shows up once again). An intuitive example would be to consider that if I were to tell you that the world is round I would not be conveying much information since presumably you already believe the world to be round with high conviction and therefore by giving you this piece of information (and assuming you consider me a reliable source) I do not resolve much uncertainty for you. If however I were to tell you that the St. Louis Cardinals won the baseball game today that, being something you are likely much less certain about, would convey a bit of information.

But why then does the string 1001000111 convey more information than the string 1111100000? This is where Shannon's insights begin to yield practical technology. For the engineering problem, the meaning of the message is irrelevant and all that matters is the likelihood of the different symbols potentially coming across the wire. The probabilities of different symbols coming across the wire are a function of the properties of the source sending the signals. For example, if the message source sends messages in English then we know that the likelihood of the letter "e" coming across the wire is much higher than the likelihood of the letter "q" coming across the wire and if the letter "q" does come across the wire we can be almost certain that the letter "u" will be the next letter to come across the wire.

Shannon observed that these variations in probability can be harnessed to condense messages by properly encoding messages before they are transmitted. Now regarding our two messages above, I must modify my claim that one message contains more information than the other message since it depends on the nature of the message source. Suppose the message source is conveying the results of coin tosses with 1 indicating the coin landed on heads and 0 tails. In this case, getting the message 1111100000 would convey the same amount of information as the message 1001000111. Both messages are in fact equally likely and the fact that in one case we landed five heads followed by five tails would be merely coincidence.

But the reason I claimed earlier that the message 1111100000 contained less information is because such highly correlated symbols raise the likelihood that the data source is not conveying equiprobable possibilities independent from each other with each sent symbol. Rather it is likely that the nature of the data source is such that if a 1 is sent the likelihood that a 1 is sent again increases. For example, perhaps 1 means it rained today and 0 means that it didn't. It is well known that whether it rains or not on a given day is positively correlated with whether it rained the day before. Because of this correlation between symbols, I know something about the likelihood of the next symbol based on the prior one and therefore when the symbol comes through it resolves less uncertainty than if I knew nothing. What Shannon showed is that if such correlation exists one can take advantage of them when encoding the message for transmission to shorten the average length of messages conveyed from the data source. To give a very simplistic example, in this case you may encode your message by sending a "1" and then simply sending how many times "1" repeats itself until the next symbol is 0. Therefore we could shorten 1111100000 to 1505. But if we used this scheme on the other message we would get 1102110313 which is no shorter than the original message. We've seen the power of convexity, or the tendency for small asymmetries in uncertain transactions to compound into predictable macro effects. Shannon would do something far more dramatic than merely observe this tendency. He would deliberately engineer it.

The challenge Shannon had was to figure the most efficient way to transmit information across a wire. Just as with Carnot's study of steam engines, electronic transmission had been around for a while when Shannon came to study it. And just as Carnot's theoretical work—which defined the upper limits of engine efficiency—eventually guided vast improvements in engine design, Shannon's theoretical work would yield similarly world-altering practical consequences, moving mere information transmission into the world of computing.

The difficulty Shannon faced was that the message received by the receiver deviated to some degree from the message that was sent by the sender. These errors were caused by noise, or interference that affected the signal while in transmission between the sender and receiver. Practitioners of the time already had various means of combating this problem including increasing the amplitude of the signal, better insulating the wire, or sending the message multiple times so that the receiver could compare the messages and repair erroneous symbols. However these methods were expensive, slow, never guaranteed a perfect transmission and sometimes required a human to mediate.

What Shannon realized was that instead of focusing on the channel and attempting to make the conditions perfect such that the message could pass through unimpeded, one could achieve perfect results simply by shaping the message appropriately. By encoding it properly one could cause the message to rise above the noise and be received in perfect condition regardless of how noisy the channel was—so long as the rate of transmission stayed below a specific limit, a limit Shannon proved how to calculate for any given channel. This was a completely unanticipated result, hardly believable by practitioners of the time.

Consider how radical this claim was. Before Shannon, the assumption was that perfect transmission required a perfect channel. Noise was the enemy, and fighting it meant improving the physical medium—better wires, stronger signals, more shielding. Shannon showed that perfect transmission could be achieved over an imperfect channel. You didn't need to fix the pipe. You needed to shape the water.

Here we see, I believe, the same structural insight that Boltzmann arrived at when he abandoned his first attempt to explain entropy. Recall that Boltzmann initially tried to derive entropy from meticulous Newtonian mechanics at the atomic level—and failed. Only when he stepped back and thought in terms of distributions and asymmetries did the answer emerge. Shannon's contemporaries were doing the equivalent of Boltzmann's first attempt: trying to solve noise by controlling the channel at the physical level. Shannon, like Boltzmann, found his answer at a higher level of abstraction. Instead of fighting the noise directly, he asked: given that noise is random and the message is structured, can the structure be made robust enough that the randomness cannot destroy it? The answer was yes—and the mechanism is the same compounding of small asymmetries we have been calling convexity.

I should be transparent that Shannon and information theorists would not typically describe the key ideas in terms of convexity. The standard vocabulary involves concepts like the asymptotic equipartition property, typicality, and random coding arguments—descendants of the law of large numbers more than of convexity per se. But what I want to draw your attention to is the common structure beneath the surface. Shannon's proof that error can be driven to zero works because of the same exponential mathematics that makes Boltzmann's entropy work: when you have many independent trials and a small asymmetry favoring one outcome over another, the favorable outcome doesn't just edge ahead—it dominates, and it dominates with a force that grows exponentially with the number of trials. Whether we call this convexity or large deviations or the law of large numbers, the underlying shape is the same curve we have been studying: small biases compounding across many uncertain transactions into overwhelming macro effects. That this same structure appears in thermodynamics, in information theory, and—as we will see—in scripture is the observation that inspired this book.

Let me show you how the compounding works in Shannon's case.

Encoding does two key things. It compresses the message to reduce the energy and bandwidth needed to send it, and it adds redundancy to counteract the noise in the channel. I've shown above an example of compression. The second function—redundancy—is where the convex structure becomes visible.

Redundancy is often considered inefficient, but it is also a major tool for building convex positions. Consider sending a single bit—a 0 or a 1—over a noisy channel which has a 10% chance of flipping the bit to the other symbol. If you send it only once you will have a 10% chance of receiving the wrong message and not knowing it. Now suppose instead you send the same bit three times and let the receiver take a majority vote. The message will be received incorrectly only if two or more of the three copies are flipped. The probability of this happening is about 2.8%—a significant improvement over 10%, but not as dramatic as you might hope from tripling your effort.

But here is where the exponential machinery kicks in. Shannon showed that with cleverer encoding schemes—not simple repetition but mathematically sophisticated codes that spread redundancy across long blocks of symbols—the probability of error doesn't just decrease. It decreases exponentially with the length of the code. Double the block length and the error rate doesn't halve; it might drop by a factor of a thousand. This is the convex signature: each additional unit of redundancy buys you disproportionately more reliability than the last. The asymmetry between the structured message and the random noise gets amplified with every additional symbol until the noise is effectively powerless.

What is crucial to understand is that Shannon proved this was possible—that codes exist which can achieve error rates as close to zero as desired, for any channel with noise below a calculable threshold—but he did not initially construct those codes. His was an existence proof, a theoretical guarantee that the mathematics permitted what engineers had assumed was impossible. It would take decades for the codes themselves to be developed, and the story of that development—from Hamming codes to turbo codes to the modern LDPC codes that underpin your cell phone—is a story of engineers gradually learning to build encoding schemes that approach the theoretical limit Shannon defined. Shannon set the speed of light, and the field spent fifty years building ways to approach it.

In other words, encoding eliminates redundancy in the message itself through compression, and then adds redundancy of its own, structured and optimized to reduce error to a chosen frequency. The first operation removes the natural, haphazard redundancy of the source. The second adds artificial, precisely engineered redundancy tuned to the specific noise characteristics of the channel. This is not the brute-force redundancy of a person on a bad phone connection repeating themselves—it is redundancy shaped with surgical precision, designed to extract the maximum possible error correction from the minimum possible added length and it happens without any human intervention.

Whereas Carnot's theoretical framework revealed how engines harness the tendency of heat to flow toward equilibrium—temperature differences doing work as they equalize—Shannon's framework revealed how to harness the opposite tendency: how to perpetuate a disequilibrium, keeping a structured message distinct from the chaos around it, indefinitely. The engine extracts work from a system as it moves toward disorder. Encoding preserves order within a system that is trying (or will tend) to destroy it. Both depend on the same mathematics of entropy. Both exploit the asymmetry between structure and randomness. Both extract practical value from the compounding of small probabilistic advantages.

Before Shannon, the telegraph was digital. The telephone was analog. The difference mattered enormously. An analog signal degrades continuously—each relay, each mile of wire strips away fidelity that can never be fully recovered. A digital signal either arrives intact or it doesn't, and Shannon showed that with proper encoding, it could always arrive intact. This insight is what made modern digital communication possible. Not because Shannon invented the idea of sending discrete symbols—Morse had done that a century earlier—but because Shannon proved that discrete symbols, properly encoded, could be transmitted with perfect fidelity across channels of any noisiness, up to a computable limit. Almost overnight, the problem of noise was transformed from an unsolvable physical limitation into a solved mathematical one.

Shannon even calculated the exact capacity of a channel—the maximum rate at which information could be transmitted with arbitrarily low error, given the channel's bandwidth and noise level. This is the Shannon limit, and it stands as a kind of boundary law for communication in the same way Carnot's limit stands as a boundary law for engines. Both men defined the ceiling. Both ceilings turned out to be astonishingly generous—far more was possible than practitioners had assumed.

Now, step back and observe the parallel. Boltzmann showed that the second law of thermodynamics is not a deterministic law enforced by Newtonian mechanics but a probabilistic certainty arising from the overwhelming number of microstates corresponding to equilibrium. Shannon showed that perfect communication is not a physical feat requiring a perfect channel but a probabilistic certainty arising from efficient redundancy overcoming the noise that would corrupt the message. In both cases, the key move is the same: stop trying to control individual particles or individual bits and instead recognize that when the space of possibilities is vast, even tiny structural asymmetries become decisive. Boltzmann realized that equilibrium wins not because physics demands it but because the deck is stacked in its favor by combinatorics. Shannon realized that the message wins not because the channel is clean but because the code stacks the deck in the message's favor by the same combinatorial logic.

This is the deep connection I want to draw out—not that Shannon consciously borrowed from Boltzmann, though he certainly adopted Boltzmann's entropy formula (Shannon's definition of information entropy is mathematically identical to Boltzmann's thermodynamic entropy, a fact that delighted and puzzled physicists for decades), but that both men solved their respective problems by recognizing the same structural truth. When you cannot control the micro-level chaos, you shape the macro-level object and let the chaos work for you. When the space of possibilities is large enough, small asymmetries don't just help—they overwhelm. This is convexity in its most potent form.

The practical insight we drew from Boltzmann at the end of the last chapter bears repeating here because Shannon's work is its most stunning vindication. When attempting to achieve something within the midst of a complex and volatile environment, instead of attempting to control the environment, or compute through the complexity, one can be very effective merely by shaping the single object that is of interest and then letting the combobulation of the environment ratchet the object towards the objective. Boltzmann showed this was how nature worked. Shannon showed it could be engineered. In the next chapter, we will see that scripture has been describing this same strategy all along—and applying it not to gas molecules or digital messages, but to the human soul.

Interestingly, the two disciplines that have most deeply pierced the boundary between order and chaos have both yielded tremendously practical technology. The relationship between order and chaos has been the subject of myth across many cultures, and thermodynamics and information theory represent the first scientific successes in understanding and harnessing its dynamics. That both sciences arrived at the same structural insight—that small asymmetries, compounded across vast numbers of uncertain interactions, produce overwhelming and predictable macro effects—is itself a pattern worth noticing. It is the pattern this book is about.

### Absorbing Barriers

Hopefully you have gained an intuitive feel by now for the idea of symmetry breaking, and how it functions to allow complexity and form to emerge from homogeneity. Small deviations from symmetry begin to cause further asymmetries which combine and compound until major separations emerge and symmetry gives way to difference.

But its not this simple either. Difference doesn’t usually compound. Difference usually dissapates. Even in cases where difference accumulates initially - the brazil nuts rise to the top - what then? For these distinctions to find novel traction in cooperation with other accumulated distinctions requires enormous scale, possibility, variance and interaction, as well as an enormous amount of nothing happening in most cases. It is telling that we still have not created a programmatic, simulated environment where interesting, novel order arises naturally over long time periods. I’m not saying we won’t get there, in fact I look forward to the time we do create such a simulated world, but that we can’t do it yet tells us something about the specialness of long-term accumulated order. It doesn’t simply arise by mixing a bunch of stuff together and stirring.

The reason for this is absorbing barriers.

If my lawn dies completely, no amount of watering will revive it. If my investment portfolio balance reaches zero, there is no positive return that can increase the balance. In the computer simulations from the previous chapter, if the entire space becomes filled with either light or dark cells, no pattern can ever emerge again. These are all examples of absorbing barriers—states within a system which, if attained, can never be escaped.

The concept is simple but its consequences are profound. Consider a gambler who starts with $100 and plays a fair game—each round he has a 50% chance of winning a dollar and a 50% chance of losing a dollar. On average, his expected outcome on any given round is zero. But the game is not symmetric in practice, because $0 is an absorbing barrier. If his balance ever hits zero, the game is over. He cannot bet what he does not have. And so even in a perfectly fair game, given enough rounds, the gambler is guaranteed to go broke. The absorbing barrier at the bottom makes an otherwise symmetric game fatally asymmetric. Over time, it devours every player. It is a concave game.

The critical feature of an absorbing barrier is that it is irreversible. It doesn't matter how well things were going before, or how favorable the odds might have been—once the barrier is reached, the system is trapped. This is what makes absorbing barriers fundamentally different from ordinary setbacks. A setback is a loss you can recover from. An absorbing barrier is a loss that ends the game.

In the brazil nut example, the brazil nuts reaching the top could also be considered an absorbing barrier, since once they have fully separated from the peanuts no amount of shaking will ever remix them. Here the absorbing barrier works in your favor—it locks in a desirable outcome. But more often in life, the absorbing barriers we encounter are the dangerous kind: the ones that lock in ruin. Death is the most obvious. Addiction can function as one. Certain financial, relational, and spiritual failures can operate this way too—not because recovery is theoretically impossible, but because the system, left to its own devices, provides no mechanism for escape. The state, once entered, is self-reinforcing.

It is this concept—the absorbing barrier—that transforms the story of the Fall from a quaint theological parable into a precise description of the human predicament. And it is the elimination of the absorbing barrier that reveals what the Atonement actually accomplishes.

### The Fortunate Fall

In 1667 John Milton published Paradise Lost, an epic ten-thousand-line poem about the Biblical story of Adam and Eve. ### Entering Moral Space

The obvious strategy is to attempt to understand enough as fast as possible to get by, to stay alive, and then once we have enough knowledge eventually prosper. But will this work for Adam and Eve? It wouldn't work for a baby entering the world.

Consider another metaphor. Imagine the pilot of a small airplane you are riding in suddenly has a heart attack and dies, leaving you to fend for yourself. It is up to you to fly the plane and eventually, before you run out of fuel, find a suitable landing area and safely land the aircraft. The airplane is perfectly functional and you are a healthy, reasonably competent person, so on the one hand you have everything you need at your fingertips. And yet, it is unlikely that you'll be able to do it. And if you fail, it is unlikely that you'll ever get another chance—crashing is an absorbing barrier. It is possible that everything will go perfectly, you'll intuit the needed actions in real time, and by luck spot a convenient landing place. It's possible, but not likely. And if there are any complicating variables like bad weather, low visibility, unfriendly terrain, or low fuel, that likelihood becomes grim. If you've ever spent time playing flight simulators, you know firsthand how small errors can compound until the airplane is out of control. This is the prospect Adam and Eve faced leaving the Garden.

These scenarios are extremely concave. The impact of uncertainty is asymmetric, but the asymmetry is against you. Errors have big consequences, and non-errors do little to make up for errors. There theoretically is a path to success, but if that path is not identified quickly and followed precisely, deviations from it will send one branching off into the absorbing barriers on either side. To quote Matthew, "narrow is the way, which leadeth unto life, and few there be that find it." There is perhaps no better description of an absorbing barrier than eternal damnation—eternal non-progress.

The thing is, this isn't just about Adam and Eve. At some point each one of us wakes up and finds ourselves outside the Garden of Eden—outside the boundaries of the place where our needs are automatically met—and we must try to understand how to stay alive and how to find joy. Action is needed NOW, but understanding cannot come until later. Full understanding much, much later—much, much too late. This is the fundamental problem of existence. We are required to act now even though knowledge comes later. Action under uncertainty.

### The Concavity of our Position toward the Law

Strikingly, Lehi anticipates that the natural response will be to look for the rulebook and figure out how to act in harmony with the forces at play. Regarding this tendency he gives telling advice:

And by the law no flesh is justified; or, by the law men are cut off. Yea, by the temporal law they were cut off; and also, by the spiritual law they perish from that which is good, and become miserable forever. (2 Nephi 2:5)

According to Lehi, the very thing which we think might be capable of saving us—the law—is instead the means by which we are cut off. If we think of the law as representing base reality, then it is obedience to the law—actions in harmony with reality, with physics if we want to be ultra-reductionist—which ought to be how we avoid destruction. How can Lehi claim that the law will be our downfall instead of our savior?

It's kind of like saying that the laws of physics will be why planes crash instead of why planes fly—it depends on the plane and on who's flying it. For Adam and Eve and for each of us, Lehi is telling us the law will be how we are cut off and not how we are exalted. Why? Because we are concave towards the Law. Errors compound. We are like a poorly piloted airplane which, given the laws of physics, will eventually make an error or series of errors from which there is no return. Each small mistake compounds on the last until we are hopelessly spiraling towards the ground.

Why are we concave towards the Law? Because death—spiritual death and physical death—are massive absorbing barriers. But more than that, it is because we are simply, as newborn moral beings, incapable of living in a full-blown moral environment. It's only a matter of time, probably a short time, until we breach the Law and become condemned. Like a baby without its mother. Like a first-time pilot without an experienced co-pilot in the next seat compensating for his mistakes, preventing them from compounding, providing the necessary grace for the student to learn.

Remember the gambler from our discussion of absorbing barriers—how even a fair game becomes fatal when $0 is an inescapable floor. Our situation before the Law is worse than that gambler's. The game of moral life is not merely fair—it is rigged against the beginner. We enter moral space as amoral infants, and the absorbing barrier of spiritual death waits below us with infinite patience. We don't have to be terrible. We just have to be imperfect, and we have to be imperfect only once in a way that compounds beyond recovery.

The Law is the dimensional backbone upon which moral space—the space of happiness and misery—hangs. This makes the math cut and dry. So long as we act in accordance with the Law we can stay alive physically and spiritually and even make small progress towards expanding ourselves to be equal with our new, moral environment. But missteps carry consequences, and it is only a matter of time before those consequences entrap us within the absorbing barrier of death—physical or spiritual. And then we're out of the game. At first look, Eve's decision to partake of the fruit appears to have been a massively concave, massively foolish bet. If only wives would listen to their husbands!

### The Stumbling Stone

Lehi is not alone in preaching this view. Paul explains the same idea—that the Law, rather than being a means of salvation, is a stumbling block:

What shall we say then? That the Gentiles, which followed not after righteousness, have attained to righteousness, even the righteousness which is of faith. But Israel, which followed after the law of righteousness, hath not attained to the law of righteousness. Wherefore? Because they sought it not by faith, but as it were by the works of the law. For they stumbled at that stumblingstone. (Romans 9:30–32)

Paul describes the error of those who depend on the law for salvation, even going so far as to say that those without the Law may end up being better off. The Gentiles, which followed not after righteousness, have attained to righteousness. The Jews of Christ's day were the textbook example of mistaking meticulous adherence to the law as the path to salvation, not realizing the unresolvable concavity of their strategy.

Notice how Boltzmann traversed this same path. His first attempt to explain entropy was the precise, computational approach—meticulous modeling of individual atomic interactions using Newton's laws. It failed. Maxwell's demon and Loschmidt's reversibility paradox demolished it. Only when Boltzmann abandoned the attempt to derive macro behavior from micro rules—only when he stepped back and thought in terms of distributions, probabilities, and asymmetries—did the answer emerge. Lehi and Paul are warning against the same error applied to the soul. DO NOT, they advise, try to read the rulebook and compute your behavior. Trying to live our lives that way is like a NASCAR driver relying on a physics textbook to learn how to drive. Except the scriptures are not a textbook—it is our tendency to treat them like one which becomes the problem.

### Grace as Convex Transformation

If the law condemns us, where do we turn to find a way through? Lehi continues:

Wherefore, redemption cometh in and through the Holy Messiah; for he is full of grace and truth. Behold, he offereth himself a sacrifice for sin, to answer the ends of the law, unto all those who have a broken heart and a contrite spirit; and unto none else can the ends of the law be answered. (2 Nephi 2:6–7)

While Lehi reminding us that we are saved by the Grace of Christ is not a new idea, to consider it on the heels of having learned that the Law is our condemnation is provocative. We can start to get a glimpse of how grace enters into the picture. Grace saves us by protecting us from the rigidity of the law—it saves us by ensuring we don't die. Grace transforms our concave position before the law into convex possibility by blocking access, at least temporarily, to the absorbing barrier of spiritual death.

Think of what this means in the precise terms we have been developing. The absorbing barrier is what makes life concave—it is the reason errors compound fatally, the reason the game ends before the learning can take hold. Grace removes the absorbing barrier. Or rather, grace interposes itself between us and the barrier, the way a co-pilot interposes himself between a student and a fatal crash. The Law—reality itself—is full of possibility so long as it doesn't kill us first. Grace keeps the plane in the air until we can learn to fly it ourselves. But unlike an airplane which has a limit on the upside—it can only fly so far on a tank of fuel—life is open-ended, possibility is infinite if we can find it. In particular, life contains the potential for beauty most will ever only realize a minute fraction of, and which the world in general is forgetting exists altogether.

One of the biggest paradoxes of Christianity is the debate between being saved by works and being saved by grace. At the heart of this paradox is the role that obedience plays in salvation. At one extreme we have the doctrine of predestination which asserts that works are completely impotent in justifying salvation and that salvation is delivered exclusively by the grace of Christ. At the other extreme we have the assertion that faith without works, as James says, is dead, and that works are central to qualifying for the saving grace of Christ. C.S. Lewis weighed in, giving his now famous metaphor of works and grace being like the blades on a pair of scissors—which blade is doing the cutting? While this metaphor at least indicates the legitimacy of salvation requiring both grace and works, it fails to give much understanding as to precisely how the two ideas interact. It has no asymmetry. Certainly grace and works are not as identical in their function as the blades of a pair of scissors.

Given a symmetry-breaking view of creation, we can make real headway resolving this problem. Grace transforms life from a concave position where works can never overcome sin, into a convex position, by erasing the effects of sin if we will repent. Sin becomes an occasion for repentance and growth while the upside that works can achieve remains fully intact. Works do the heavy lifting of personal exaltation; the Grace of Christ grants the required space for those meager works to take root and eventually compound. Obedience to the law is not ineffective—it is rather enabled by grace. It is grace that renders truth usable.

Return to our gambler, but now change the rules. Suppose a benefactor steps in and declares that the gambler's balance can never fall below $1. Every time he would otherwise hit zero, the benefactor covers the loss. The game is transformed. The absorbing barrier is gone. Now the gambler can play indefinitely, and if the game offers even a slight edge—even the faintest asymmetry in his favor—that edge will compound over time into something substantial. What was once a death sentence becomes an open field. The odds haven't changed. The rules haven't changed. What changed is that someone absorbed the downside. This is what grace does. And notice: the gambler must still play. The benefactor doesn't play for him. Grace does not replace works—it transforms the geometry within which works operate.

The Grace of Christ comes in many different ways. Each unearned breath, each sunrise, every cool desert breeze—the general givenness of daily existence, the good as well as the bad—each of these can be appreciated as a grace, and this recognition alone is enough to transform one's soul. But the full portion of Christ's grace is discovered only when it is sought directly. "Ask, and it shall be given you; seek, and ye shall find; knock, and it shall be opened unto you: For every one that asketh receiveth; and he that seeketh findeth; and to him that knocketh it shall be opened." (Matthew 7:7–8) The Grace of Christ not only answers the law, making it a means of salvation rather than damnation, but grants us new perspective, new awareness—allows us to begin to see as moral beings, a trait which will prove rather critical as we leave separation behind.

The grace of Christ transforms sin from an absorbing barrier into an occasion for repentance—which is to say an occasion for change and growth.

### How Grace Works

We have established that grace transforms life from a concave position to a convex one by blocking the absorbing barrier of spiritual death. But this formulation, while correct, is too clean. It sounds like flipping a switch—concave becomes convex, problem solved. To understand why grace matters as deeply as it does, and why the Atonement is not merely a nice theological feature but the structural prerequisite for human existence in moral space, we need to look more carefully at what sin actually does, what repentance actually is, and what it means for Christ to "answer the ends of the law."

Start with sin. The calculation-condition view of sin is that it is a discrete event: you broke a rule, you incurred a penalty, the penalty must be paid. This is the ledger-book model, and it is not wrong so much as it is catastrophically incomplete. It is like describing an avalanche as "some snow moved downhill." True, but it misses everything that matters about avalanches.

What sin actually does—mechanically, in the terms we have been developing—is alter the conditions under which all subsequent decisions are made. A single lie does not merely add one black mark to a cosmic scorecard. It changes the liar. Having lied once, the liar now has something to protect. The next interaction is no longer conducted by the same person operating under the same conditions. It is conducted by a person who has a secret, who must now track what they said and to whom, who must calculate how to keep the false version of events consistent. The cognitive and emotional overhead of maintaining the lie drains resources that would otherwise be available for honest engagement. Worse, the lie often demands further lies to sustain itself. A second deception is easier than the first, not because the moral gravity of lying has lessened but because the liar has already absorbed the fixed costs of living inauthentically and the marginal cost of one more lie has dropped.

This is compounding working against you. Each moral failure doesn't merely add to a total—it changes the shape of the person making subsequent decisions, making further failure more likely. A betrayal of trust doesn't just damage the relationship in which it occurred; it erodes the betrayer's own capacity for trust, which is the very faculty upon which future moral action depends. Someone who has cheated once has demonstrated to themselves that they are the kind of person who cheats, and this self-knowledge—even when suppressed—bends the probabilities of every future choice. Resentment nursed becomes bitterness. Bitterness becomes cynicism. Cynicism becomes cruelty. None of these transitions requires a dramatic moral collapse. Each one is a small shift in the asymmetry, tilting the person incrementally toward the absorbing barrier.

This is why sin is so much more dangerous than the ledger-book model suggests. A ledger tracks debits and credits as independent entries. But sin is not independent. It is autocorrelated, like weather—whether it rained today changes the probability of rain tomorrow. And unlike weather, which fluctuates around a mean, the autocorrelation of sin tends in one direction. Each failure makes the next failure marginally more available, more familiar, more tolerable. The asymmetry is concave. You are the airplane in the hands of a novice pilot, and each uncorrected deviation tightens the spiral.

This is also why mere willpower—what we might call the Adamic strategy of reading the rulebook harder—cannot solve the problem. Willpower operates on the current decision. But the current decision is being made by a person whose shape has already been altered by prior decisions. You cannot will yourself into being the person you were before the sin any more than you can will the airplane back to level flight once the spiral has begun by simply gripping the yoke harder. The instrument you are gripping with—yourself—is the very thing that has been compromised.

Now consider repentance.

If sin is the progressive deformation of one's shape such that the asymmetry compounds toward the absorbing barrier, repentance is the reshaping of the person such that the asymmetry begins compounding in the other direction. This is a critical distinction. Repentance is not rewinding the tape. It is not undoing what was done. The lies were told. The trust was broken. The person you were before the sin no longer exists and cannot be recovered, any more than the shaken jar of nuts can be returned to its initial configuration. Repentance does not reverse history. It reorients the present.

Think of the brazil nut. It rises not because someone reaches in and places it at the top, but because its shape—its size relative to the peanuts, its density relative to gravity—creates an asymmetry that, across many transactions of shaking, compounds into upward movement. If you could somehow deform a brazil nut into the size and shape of a peanut, it would stop rising. It would diffuse into the mass like everything else. Sin is this kind of deformation. It reshapes the person into something that no longer catches the upward currents of moral life. The sinner does not lack opportunity—moral space is as full of possibility as ever—but their shape no longer engages with it productively. They have been rounded down to something that the shaking moves sideways or pushes further down rather than lifting up.

Repentance, then, is the restoration of the shape. Not the original shape—that is gone—but a shape that once again catches the asymmetry in the right direction. The repentant liar does not become a person who never lied. They become a person who, having lied, has reoriented so fundamentally toward honesty that the very memory of dishonesty becomes a kind of ballast—a weight on the side of truth that a person who had never lied could not possess. This is why repentance can produce people who are in some sense stronger than the innocent. The shape is different, but the asymmetry it creates can be even more pronounced. The reformed addict who has stared into the absorbing barrier and been pulled back understands the stakes of sobriety with a vividness the person who never struggled cannot access.

But here is the crux: repentance, by itself, is not enough. Repentance is reshaping, but reshaping only matters if you are still in the game. If the absorbing barrier has already been reached—if the spiral has already terminated in spiritual death—then it does not matter how profoundly the person is reshaped. A gambler with perfect insight into probability theory is no better off if his balance is already zero. You cannot compound from nothing. You cannot rise from a floor that has swallowed you.

This is where the Atonement enters, and this is what it means to say that Christ "answers the ends of the law." The law operates with perfect mechanical consistency—every cause has its effect, every sin its consequence, every spiral its terminus. The law is not cruel; it is simply precise. And its precision means that at some point the compounding of sin reaches a threshold beyond which the person cannot generate the resources needed to repent. They see the error. They may even ache to change. But the deformation is too severe, the spiral too advanced, the distance to a functional shape too great to traverse under their own power. This is spiritual death—not a punishment imposed from outside, but the natural terminal state of unchecked concavity. It is the absorbing barrier doing what absorbing barriers do: absorbing.

What Christ does in the Atonement is not undo the sin. The lie was still told. The trust was still broken. The consequences in the world still ripple outward. What Christ does is absorb the terminal consequence—the one that would end the game. He stands between the sinner and the absorbing barrier and says: not yet. You are not done. The floor holds. Now reshape.

The simplicity of this formulation should not disguise its profundity. By absorbing the terminal consequence, Christ does not merely offer a second chance. He transforms the entire structure of moral life from a system where errors compound toward an inescapable end into a system where errors become the occasion for reorientation and growth. This is the concave-to-convex transformation described with full mechanical precision. The law still operates. Consequences still exist. Sin still deforms. But the deformation is no longer terminal, which means repentance is no longer futile, which means the reshaping can begin, which means the asymmetry can be restored, which means the compounding can resume in the right direction. Grace does not suspend the law. It changes what the law's consequences are capable of doing to you.

There is no better account of what this feels like from the inside than Alma's recollection in the 36th chapter of the book of Alma. Alma the Younger had spent years actively fighting against the church his father had built, and was, by any honest reckoning, deep in the spiral. He describes his condition with the unflinching specificity of a man who has not romanticized what happened to him:

"I was racked with eternal torment, for my soul was harrowed up to the greatest degree and racked with all my sins. Yea, I did remember all my sins and iniquities, for which I was tormented with the pains of hell; yea, I saw that I had rebelled against my God, and that I had not kept his holy commandments." (Alma 36:12–13)

Note what Alma is describing. This is not abstract guilt. This is the felt experience of a person confronting the full topology of their own deformation—seeing, all at once, the compound effect of every deviation, every choice that bent the shape further from what it should have been. He is looking at the absorbing barrier and recognizing that he has reached it. The "pains of hell" are not fire and brimstone imposed by a vengeful God. They are the experience of being a system that has arrived at a state from which, under the law's own logic, there is no exit. This is spiritual death experienced from the inside.

And then:

"And it came to pass that as I was thus racked with torment, while I was harrowed up by the memory of my many sins, behold, I remembered also to have heard my father prophesy unto the people concerning the coming of one Jesus Christ, a Son of God, to atone for the sins of the world. Now, as my mind caught hold upon this thought, I cried within my heart: O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me, who am in the gall of bitterness, and am encircled about by the everlasting chains of death." (Alma 36:17–18)

This is the moment of reorientation—the beginning of repentance. Notice that Alma does not resolve anything himself. He does not formulate a plan. He does not take inventory of his sins and devise a strategy for correcting each one. He calls out. He does the one thing a person at the absorbing barrier can do, which is ask for the barrier to give way.

"And now, behold, when I thought this, I could remember my pains no more; yea, I was harrowed up by the memory of my sins no more. And oh, what joy, and what marvelous light I did behold; yea, my soul was filled with joy as exceeding as was my pain!" (Alma 36:19–20)

What Alma describes here is not the completion of repentance. It is the restoration of possibility. The absorbing barrier has released him. The floor has reformed beneath his feet. He has not yet done the work of reshaping—that will take years, and will constitute the rest of his life. But the precondition for that reshaping has been met: he is still in the game. Grace did not fix him. Grace gave him back the capacity to be fixed—to be reshaped, to restore the asymmetry, to begin compounding in the right direction again. The joy he felt was not the joy of completion. It was the joy of a man who, having hit the ground, discovers the ground is not the end.

Notice too the symmetry Alma insists upon: "my soul was filled with joy as exceeding as was my pain." This is not a coincidence and it is not poetic license. It is a precise description of what happens when an absorbing barrier is converted into a floor. The same depth that made the fall so terrible is what makes the recovery so profound. The person who has never approached the barrier cannot experience this particular joy, because they have never felt the full weight of what the barrier means. The dimensionality of moral space cuts in both directions—the same polarity that makes damnation possible is what makes this kind of redemption possible. This is Lehi's point about opposition in all things expressed not as theology but as lived experience.

Now return to the gambler, with whom we introduced absorbing barriers earlier in this chapter. The gambler whose benefactor guarantees he will never hit zero does not play the same game as the gambler without that guarantee, even though the rules of each hand are identical. The unguaranteed gambler must play conservatively—must hoard chips, avoid risk, calculate frantically—because a single bad run can end everything. The guaranteed gambler can play with a fundamentally different posture. He can take risks that the unguaranteed gambler cannot afford, because the downside is bounded. Over time, if the game offers even the slightest edge, this freedom to engage without existential terror means the guaranteed gambler will find the edge and exploit it, compounding his position upward while the unguaranteed gambler, paralyzed by the proximity of zero, never compounds at all.

But notice: the benefactor does not play the hands. The gambler still plays. The gambler still makes decisions. The gambler can still play badly in any given hand. The guarantee does not confer skill—it confers the conditions under which skill can be developed. This is the relationship between grace and works made mechanically precise. Grace is not a substitute for works. It is the condition that makes works non-futile. Without grace, every work is shadowed by the absorbing barrier—every good act is undertaken in the knowledge that a single sufficiently bad act can undo everything, forever. With grace, works can accumulate. They can compound. Small acts of faith, repeated across a lifetime, can build upon each other because the floor is guaranteed and the ceiling is infinite.

This is what "enduring to the end" means in convexity terms. It does not mean maintaining a white-knuckled, unblemished record of perfect obedience—that is the calculation condition reasserting itself in pious disguise. It means continuing to play. It means continuing to show up to the table, hand after hand, reshaping when you've been deformed, reorienting when you've drifted, trusting that the floor will hold even when—especially when—you have just played the worst hand of your life. The promise is not that you will never lose a hand. The promise is that losing a hand is never the end.

Christ's grace is not a single intervention. It is a standing offer—perpetual, inexhaustible, operative in every moment for as long as the person keeps playing. This is why the sacrament is weekly and not annual. It is why prayer is daily and not occasional. These are not rituals of obligation. They are the recurring points at which the person reconfirms that they are still in the game, still oriented, still willing to be reshaped. Each one is an act of what Shannon would call re-encoding: stripping the noise that has accumulated since the last transmission and reinforcing the signal of who you are becoming against the interference of who you have been. The floor holds. The game continues. The compounding resumes.

### Opposition in All Things

It turns out that to point to science and then to the Bible is to point to only a double coincidence when in fact what we are dealing with is an exponentially rarer triple coincidence. The Book of Mormon too addresses symmetry breaking—and not by so simple a mechanism as mimicking the Bible. In the very same chapter we've been reading from all along, Lehi not only describes asymmetry as the foundation of existence but, unlike the Bible, calls attention to it explicitly and describes why it is important. Not only this, Lehi covers cosmological territory which the Bible skips altogether. I've decided to quote the next key verses in full. Before you read them, pause, collect yourself, clear your mind, and prepare to enjoy them. They are a grace.

For it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things. If not so, my firstborn in the wilderness, righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness, neither holiness nor misery, neither good nor bad. Wherefore, all things must needs be a compound in one; wherefore, if it should be one body it must needs remain as dead, having no life neither death, nor corruption nor incorruption, happiness nor misery, neither sense nor insensibility.

Wherefore, it must needs have been created for a thing of naught; wherefore there would have been no purpose in the end of its creation. Wherefore, this thing must needs destroy the wisdom of God and his eternal purposes, and also the power, and the mercy, and the justice of God.

And if ye shall say there is no law, ye shall also say there is no sin. If ye shall say there is no sin, ye shall also say there is no righteousness. And if there be no righteousness there be no happiness. And if there be no righteousness nor happiness there be no punishment nor misery. And if these things are not there is no God. And if there is no God we are not, neither the earth; for there could have been no creation of things, neither to act nor to be acted upon; wherefore, all things must have vanished away.

And now, my sons, I speak unto you these things for your profit and learning; for there is a God, and he hath created all things, both the heavens and the earth, and all things that in them are, both things to act and things to be acted upon.

And to bring about his eternal purposes in the end of man, after he had created our first parents, and the beasts of the field and the fowls of the air, and in fine, all things which are created, it must needs be that there was an opposition; even the forbidden fruit in opposition to the tree of life; the one being sweet and the other bitter.

Wherefore, the Lord God gave unto man that he should act for himself. Wherefore, man could not act for himself save it should be that he was enticed by the one or the other. (2 Nephi 2:11–16)

Morality cannot exist without enticement to opposite ends. Is there a better explanation of how polarity grants the existence of new dimensionality? To ask for a world without pain would be to ask for all things to be "compound in one." Dimensionality emerges from contrast. Ugliness is the price of beauty, bitterness the price of sweetness—not because God says so, but simply out of physical expedience. Without asymmetry—without breaks in the pattern—there's no structure, no emergence of "something."

### Eve's Sovereignty

It was Eve who, being enticed, chose to live in a world with pain, which as Lehi describes is the necessary cost of pleasure. Eve made the decision to cease being something acted upon and chose, at great cost, to become something that acts. In so doing, the final division of creation was achieved—good from evil and mankind from God.

What might we imagine was going through her mind when she accepted the forbidden fruit from Satan? You shall be as the Gods, knowing good and evil, Satan had told her. Maybe Eve saw the final division. Maybe she caught a glimpse from her two-dimensional world of what it might be like to be three-dimensional—she realized that man, without the possibility of death, could never really be alive, and she partook. It was between the extremes of death and life that man would ultimately be born as Man—as sovereign man—stretched by higher dimensionality into being. By commanding Adam and Eve not to partake of the fruit, God ensured it would be by their own agency that agency was born. God erected the dichotomy which Eve chose to invoke. Eve chose agency. Eve chose to live in a world with the possibility of catastrophic tragedy so that she and her posterity might experience breathtaking beauty. Nothing short of eternal damnation on one side can support the possibility of eternal life on the other. It is the ultimate polarity. Minimum entropy.

But what made Eve's act heroic instead of just being a reckless risk? This is what Lehi and Paul are trying to explain. To focus on obedience as the primary mechanism of salvation is to expect a baby to earn its own livelihood. This doesn't mean works are not important—they are in fact critical—but their efficacy will only have time to take root and grow if something prevents us from dying in the meantime. This is why Lehi says that the Law condemns us. If left to ourselves it certainly will. But we are not left to ourselves. It is the grace of Christ which will keep us alive.

### Adam's Hesitation

Scripturally, we get only one line from Adam regarding his thinking on the topic of Eve's initiative, but I think it does a good job summarizing his position. When God questions Adam as to how he suddenly recognized he was naked he says, "The woman whom thou gavest to be with me, she gave me of the tree, and I did eat." In other words, Adam's take is: she did it, I'm just trying to play by the rules!

The Mormon understanding of Adam is more filled out than this. In another book of Mormon scripture that relates the Garden of Eden narrative, Adam emphasizes, in a slight variation on the account in Genesis, the fact that he was in a bit of a bind. Here he states: "The woman thou gavest me, and commandest that she should remain with me, she gave me of the fruit of the tree and I did eat." You see, Lord, I can't keep her with me if she's going to be kicked out of here. Adam is thinking about all of this very logically—which is to say within the model he was explicitly given. The thing about rational thought is that it requires a firm framework within which to be rational. People suppose that there is rational thought and then there is irrational thought, but this leaves out the biggest domain of thought, which is thinking that is about domains too big to fit into frameworks yet grasped by people.

When Eve ate the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil, she did more than draw apart the polarity of good and evil. She also established a difference between herself and Adam. Interestingly, in Greek mythology Pandora, like Eve, is the first woman on earth. Similarly, she invokes chaos upon the world—not by eating tree fruit but by opening a box. Pandora's box has long been compared with Eve's apple and Pandora with Eve.

Adam did not want to eat the apple. Adam wanted to stick with the plan. Eve, glimpsing what mankind might become in a moral world, decided to call an audible and step out of the script. We see in Adam and Eve together the very paradox Lehi and Paul are explaining. Adam's insistence on adhering to the guidelines was nearly his downfall, while Eve insisted on understanding the rules by means of engagement. To Adam's credit, he ultimately chose to join with Eve.

The desire to make life about obedience is the same tendency Adam had to remain in the Garden. It is the desire for a closed game which one can confidently strategize within. Eve realized she had to blow up the game. Adam's instinct—read the rulebook, follow the instructions, stay within the known framework—is the calculation condition, and it is a losing strategy in a space too complex to model and too consequential to survive by trial and error alone.

Instead, exercise faith—acknowledge the grace by which you draw each breath, and trusting that grace, begin to act. Not in some overly logical approach, but viscerally, depending upon the grace of Christ and aggressively repenting when you go awry. Faith precedes repentance. The grace of Christ transforms sin from an absorbing barrier into an occasion for repentance, which is to say an occasion for change and growth.

### Leaving the Garden

What does it mean to leave the Garden?

And here all endowed members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints should take a moment to remember that they too have kneeled before an altar and confronted the same decision Adam and Eve had to confront, and after being given the explicit opportunity to stay, have chosen to exit the Garden. Leaving the Garden for us means the same thing it did for Adam and Eve. It means to take the reins of one's own agency, to recognize what Elder Neal A. Maxwell referred to as our "sovereign inner zone"—buried as it may be beneath genetics, culture, environment, and misaligned desire—and begin to listen to it, to give it the chief seat among all the voices vying for our attention, to nourish it, and, to use Lehi's words, to array ourselves with the things that act instead of remaining with the things that are acted upon.

We have shown how Boltzmann's breakthrough eventually led to the paradigm of symmetry breaking, and we've shown that the Book of Mormon not only points out the same pattern but describes why it matters and how we may more effectively live by understanding it—and the New Testament corroborates this reading. When attempting to achieve something within the midst of a complex and volatile environment, instead of attempting to control the environment, or compute through the complexity, one can be very effective merely by shaping the single object that is of interest and then letting the combobulation of the environment ratchet the object towards the objective. The question is not how to eliminate volatility from your life. The question is what shape you carry into it.

Eve perhaps knew this. Before there was a rulebook she looked out at the dimensionality of a moral world—its beauty, its stakes, its staggering potential—and she chose to enter it. She did not compute. She acted from the deepest thing she knew, from what we might call faith, and trusted that something larger would catch what she dropped. It did. It does. It will.

## Chapter 4: Who You Must Become

The airplane is in a flat spin. You're gripping controls you barely understand, watching the altimeter unwind toward zero. Every instinct screams to pull back on the yoke—harder—but the nose won't rise. The ground spirals closer. This is what self-improvement feels like for most people: frantic calculation, white-knuckled effort, the grinding certainty that if you could just find the right lever, master the right technique, implement the right system, you'd finally escape the spin. But the plane keeps falling. The Law—in this case the laws of physics—seems to be against you, hurling you toward destruction.

The central insight of this book is that reality operates through symmetry breaking, that grace transforms concave positions into convex ones, that life is about shaping ourselves rather than computing outcomes. If that's true, it demands we rethink self-improvement entirely. The prevailing model treats personal development as an engineering problem: diagnose deficiencies, prescribe solutions, execute protocols. It is the calculation condition applied to the soul. And like Boltzmann's first attempt to explain entropy through meticulous atomic mechanics, it fails spectacularly.

Self-improvement is not centrally about self-discipline. It is about alignment. It is about activating proclivities toward productive amplification, not suppressing them, which is all that self-discipline in isolation can do. Self-discipline is a useful tool in the short run—it incurs costs that begin to shape us until we achieve an alignment with reality that starts to break in our favor. Eventually the laws governing your fate come into focus and begin to appear useful rather than antagonistic. But discipline is a bridge and an amplifier, not a complete solution. Brute force is what is needed when you are ill aligned - physically, spiritually, philosophically, intellectually - with the world, and the power of brute force is ultimately weak and drastically insufficient. The destination is a self whose internal asymmetries compound toward what is good without requiring constant conscious intervention—the way brazil nuts rise not because each nut has been instructed where to go, but because its shape creates an asymmetry that compounds across ten thousand small interactions into an unmistakable, ordered result. This doesn’t mean coasting, it means your spirit is so alive by seeing opportunity instead of failure that you are driven to overcome.

When Adam and Eve chose to leave the Garden, they left the calm and meaningless equilibrium of Eden. By exercising agency they introduced the moral dimension to the world along with the Moral Law that attended it, creating moral space with the attending possibility of deep meaning but experienced, to new initiates, as paralyzing stakes. We cannot, as the parable teaches, succeed by burying our talents. We must engage in the world and we must engage before we are ready.

### The New Absorbing Barriers

Sin is our biggest impediment. Sin represents the absorbing barriers that threaten to pull us into a narrow, deceitfully satisfying life. The modern world provides a particular challenge in this regard, and it is worth pausing to understand why.

Technology has an interesting bias embedded in it. Technology relies on engineering. Engineering relies on logic, and logic requires adjacent ideas to build upon each other. This means our engineered technologies represent a tiny subset of what is possible—and more importantly, they are naive relative to the full breadth and depth of the problems they attempt to address. Any deployed technology targets one or only a handful of problems, and by so doing creates consequences far beyond the narrow problem it was built to solve. Consider the automobile as an improvement on the horse—targeting the problems of velocity, maintenance, and manure. Besides largely solving those problems, automobiles polluted the environment and completely transformed our cities from walkable, human-scaled places into sprawling zones designed around machines rather than people. This is not to say automobiles were a mistake. It is to demonstrate the naivete inherent in all technology and the unexpected problems that arise alongside the solutions technology provides.

There are two effects we must confront as technology marches forward and accelerates.

The first is the creation of new absorbing barriers. Technology excels at exploiting low-order satiation—the immediate, engineerable satisfactions—because these are the kind that can be optimized for. But in doing so it scatters new absorbing barriers across our landscape. Every app engineered to capture attention, every algorithm tuned to deliver frictionless pleasure, every system that removes difficulty without understanding why difficulty existed, every processed food that optimizes for taste and consistency at the expense of nobody knows—these are new walls that, once you hit them, are extraordinarily hard to escape. They don't look like walls. They look like comfort. Each time we adopt a new technology we should be examining the higher-order effects that arise precisely because the technology was not designed with the holistic role of whatever it replaced in mind. We slouch into thinness rather than compound into depth.

The second, perhaps more difficult problem, is that we forget the depth we used to have—both as individuals and as cultures.

A puzzling pattern emerged along the American frontier: thousands of white settlers, many of them women, were mixed into Native American tribes through capture, and when given the opportunity to return to colonial society, they refused. Meanwhile, Indians brought up among white colonists would seize the first opportunity to escape back to the woods with their tribe. This one-way migration haunted Benjamin Franklin and other Western thinkers because it revealed an uncomfortable truth. Despite all the material advantages of colonial society, tribal life offered something far more compelling—a sense of loyalty, belonging, and meaning that made settlers willing to abandon everything they had known. These captives had glimpsed what most people in modern society never experience: the feeling of being part of a community you would risk your life to defend, where emotional bonds run so deep that individual comfort becomes trivial by comparison. Their refusal to return stands as a quiet indictment of life organized around material accumulation rather than meaning. I would conjecture that 21st century western culture is even more devoid than the western culture of the 18th and 19th centuries.

Where there is no vision the people perish. If we cannot glimpse the profound potential of human existence, nothing can motivate us to pursue it. The absorbing barriers of the modern world are particularly insidious because they don't merely trap us—they erase our memory that anything beyond them exists. They flatten our vision of what a life can be until the flatness itself feels natural. Family dinner is one example in my own life. My own family dinners are less frequent and thinner than those I grew up with, and hearing stories from my father I know that mine where less frequent and thinner than those he had as a child.

### Shape Over Calculation

So how does one navigate? Not by computing the correct path. We have established this thoroughly. Adam's instinct—read the rulebook, follow the instructions, stay within the known framework—is the calculation condition, and it is a losing strategy in a space too complex to model and too consequential to survive by trial and error alone.

The answer the scriptures give is not a rulebook but a shape.

There is a reason the New Testament does not hand us a legal code. There is a reason the Sermon on the Mount reads more like poetry than legislation. There is a reason Christ, when pressed for the greatest commandment, gave two—love God, love your neighbor—and said all the law hangs on these. He was not offering a simplified rulebook. He was offering a shape. He was describing the orientation of a soul from which righteous action would naturally, probabilistically, almost inevitably emerge.

This is the inversion the gospel demands and which Lehi and Paul both announce: the goal is not to compute the correct action from the rules and then execute it. The goal is to become the kind of person from whom correct action flows as a matter of tendency. Not perfection in any given moment, but alignment such that across the long run of a life, across the full distribution of circumstances, your responses drift unmistakably toward joy, toward light, toward what is good. The grace of Christ is what makes this strategy viable. It blocks the absorbing barrier—the catastrophic asymmetry of spiritual death that would end the game before the compounding could begin—and transforms your life from a concave minefield into an open field possibility. Here is the key point - its not that by being righteous you feel like being moral, its that by seeing the profound possibility of creation, and understand the moral physics of obtaining that possibility, righteousness becomes automatic. Lehi and Paul are trying to tell us we’ve had obedience backwards the whole time.

Within each of us there is a spark of life that resonates with the physics, with the reality of this existence. As we discover how the phenomena of this world interacts with our soul our work turns into natural pleasure. We become mission-driven, rather than self-discipline driven, and our work adds to rather than subtracts from the world.

### The Convexity of Covenant

Paul, writing to the Ephesians, reaches for the language of structure when he describes what Christ accomplishes: "That in the dispensation of the fulness of times he might gather together in one all things in Christ, both which are in heaven, and which are on earth; even in him" (Ephesians 1:10). The Greek word rendered "gather together in one" is anakephalaiōsasthai—to sum up, to recapitulate, to bring under one head. Paul is describing not a reversal of creation's separations but their culmination in a new unity. The forces that separated are now, through Christ, finding their higher synthesis. This is not the homogenous void that preceded creation. This is the assembled Kingdom—structured, differentiated, and unified all at once, the way a fugue is unified even as each voice remains distinct.

The vehicle for this assembly is covenant.

In the theology of the Restoration, covenants are not merely agreements or promises. They are load-bearing structures. They are the mechanisms by which isolated, mortal, concave beings are connected to something large enough to absorb their volatility and convex enough to leverage it. When the Lord tells Abraham, "I will establish my covenant between me and thee and thy seed after thee" (Genesis 17:7), he is not describing a transaction. He is describing the insertion of Abraham into a system with entirely different properties than the one he inhabited before.

Consider what a covenant does to the geometry of a life. Before covenant, a person faces the moral universe largely alone—subject to the absorbing barriers Lehi describes, accumulating spiritual debt that compounds, errors that metastasize. The Law, as Lehi and Paul both insist, cannot save such a person. It can only measure how far they have fallen. This is the concave position: uncertainty works against you. Mistakes cost more than equivalent successes gain.

Covenant alters this geometry. Lehi describes grace as what "answers the ends of the law" (2 Nephi 2:7)—grace satisfies the demands of the law so that those demands no longer constitute an absorbing barrier. The spiritual death that previously waited at the bottom of every moral stumble is blocked. What was an absorbing barrier becomes a floor. A soul in covenant can repent—which is to say, can learn. Sin becomes an occasion for growth rather than a terminus. This is the convex transformation: uncertainty now works in your favor because the downside is bounded while the upside remains open.

But covenant is not merely an individual's contract with God. Its structure is inherently relational, and this relational dimension introduces a second, equally important dynamic.

The Lord, sealing the covenant with Israel at Sinai, declares: "Now therefore, if ye will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people" (Exodus 19:5). The word translated "peculiar treasure" is segullah—a personal possession, a valued property held close. This is not the language of contract enforcement. This is the language of belonging.

A single individual, mortal and concave, enters covenant relationships—with God, with a spouse, with the body of believers—and those relationships function to transform concavity into convexity before the law.

Paul, in his hymn to charity in 1 Corinthians 13, is describing exactly this. Charity "beareth all things, believeth all things, hopeth all things, endureth all things." What he is describing is a relationship so structured around the other person's highest good that the noise of mortality—their failures, their limitations, the friction of proximity—cannot ultimately corrupt the signal. Love, properly understood, is convex to suffering. Every difficulty it endures either passes or deepens it. It does not compound toward death. It compounds toward life.

A strong marriage has a convex payoff function with respect to adversity. Being single is roughly linear: you face a stressor, you absorb it yourself, and the cost scales more or less proportionally. There's no one to amplify the damage, but there's also no one to transform it.

The mechanics of a strong marriage's convexity start with specialization. Every domain of life — earning, nurturing, planning, maintaining, navigating crisis — has an S-curve: early effort yields accelerating returns, then diminishing ones. A single person must spread themselves across all of these curves simultaneously, operating in the shallow, low-slope regions of many. A strong partnership allows each person to climb higher on fewer curves — to reach the steep part where mastery compounds. The total return on invested effort across the partnership exceeds what two individuals could generate independently, for the same reason that trade between comparative-advantage nations isn't zero-sum. This is the baseline efficiency gain.

But the real convexity emerges under stress and volatility. When a shock hits — a job loss, an illness, an unexpected opportunity — the single person must respond across all dimensions at once, pulled further down every curve. The partnership can absorb and redistribute. One partner covers while the other recovers, or pivots, or doubles down. And critically, when volatility presents asymmetric upside — a risky career move, a relocation for a transformative opportunity — the partner operating high on their respective curve provides a stable platform from which the other can take the bet. The partnership doesn't just dampen downside; it captures upside that a single person, managing all curves alone, would rationally have to leave on the table. Each difficulty encountered and navigated together then yields compounding returns in trust, coordination, and mutual knowledge that exceed the cost of the stressor itself. The second derivative is positive: the marginal unit of shared hardship produces more resilience than the last.

In calm seas, the strong marriage and the single life may look comparable — the convex and linear functions are close to each other near the origin. But as volatility increases, they diverge dramatically. This is why couples who have weathered genuine adversity together often describe their bond as qualitatively different from what it was before — not merely restored to baseline, but restructured at a deeper level. The relationship doesn't just absorb volatility; it has a long position on it. Variance is, net, a friend. The single person endures. The strong marriage gains.

Of course, the converse deserves a warning label. A weak marriage isn't linear — it's concave, and steeply so. Small shocks cascade into blame, resentment, and withdrawal; the total harm exceeds the sum of its parts. The specialization that creates leverage in a strong partnership becomes dangerous dependency in a dysfunctional one — you've abandoned your position on certain curves entirely, and your partner isn't holding them either. This is the relationship that's actually worse than being single under stress — two people dragging each other below the waterline. The option value of partnership is enormous, but the sign of the curvature depends entirely on the priority of commitment each partner gives to the other.

Christ provides the foundation for the trust and alignment needed to be the basis of a convex partnership. Because convexity doesn't emerge from the mechanics of specialization alone — it requires deep mutual trust, the kind where each partner acts in the interest of the whole even when unobserved, even when defection would be easy. And trust at that depth requires common values — a shared coordinate system that both partners orient by when the fog rolls in and local optimization would tempt each one toward a different heading. Without it, the partnership becomes a principal-agent problem: you've ceded control of critical life dimensions to someone whose objective function you can't verify. That's not convexity. That's leveraged exposure to counterparty risk. Shared faith in Christ provides something that shared hobbies, shared politics, even shared temperament cannot: a common anchor that neither partner authored and neither can move. When both people are oriented toward the same transcendent standard — one that demands honesty, sacrifice, forgiveness, and covenant-keeping not because the other partner earned it but because it has already been modeled for them at infinite cost — the trust problem resolves at its root. You don't need to audit your partner's every move because you share the same loss function. And that foundation is, at least in principle, bottomless. Human alignment drifts — values shift under pressure, incentives corrode commitment, the ledger never balances. But a partnership anchored to something outside and above both partners has access to a reserve of trust that neither generated and neither can exhaust. The Atonement already priced in the worst-case scenario of human failure and covered it. That is what makes the curvature sustainable. Not willpower, not compatibility, not even love in the romantic sense — but a shared faith that provides the alignment from which everything else compounds.

### Assembly as New Creation

What then is the assembled Kingdom toward which all this tends?

The Doctrine and Covenants describes it plainly: "And that same sociality which exists among us here will exist among us there, only it will be coupled with eternal glory, which glory we do not now enjoy" (D&C 130:2).

This is striking. The assembly is not a dissolution of distinction into some undifferentiated heavenly uniformity—not a return to the symmetric void before the Big Bang. The same sociality persists, but glorified. The differentiated, distinct, particular selves that emerged through the long process of creation, fall, and mortal experience are not erased. They are gathered—each one more fully itself than it has ever been, and by virtue of being more fully itself, more capable of genuine relationship with every other. (cs lewis quote about creatures becoming more perfect as they become more different)

This corresponds, physically, to what happens when sufficient complexity has emerged through symmetry breaking to allow for new forms of order: not the original uniformity, but a structured whole greater than the sum of its differentiated parts. Life itself is such a phenomenon—carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, atoms forged in stellar furnaces and scattered across the cosmos, assembling in the specific configurations that allow a cell to metabolize, to reproduce, to perceive. None of those atoms ceased to be themselves. But their assembly created something that could not have existed in their separation.

Paul sees this when he writes of the body of Christ: "For as the body is one, and hath many members, and all the members of that one body, being many, are one body: so also is Christ" (1 Corinthians 12:12). The members do not become identical. The eye does not become an ear. The hand does not become a foot. But in their differentiated belonging to one body, each becomes more rather than less—more capable, more meaningful, more real than it could be in isolation.

The Lord's description of Zion—"every man seeking the interest of his neighbor, and doing all things with an eye single to the glory of God" (D&C 82:19)—is not a description of uniformity. It is a description of maximally convex social architecture. When each person in a community orients their gifts and efforts toward the flourishing of others, the compound effect is what Paul describes in Ephesians: the whole body "fitly joined together and compacted by that which every joint supplieth" making "increase of the body unto the edifying of itself in love" (Ephesians 4:16).

### Shaping Yourself

The practical question that haunts us, as it haunted Adam and Eve leaving the Garden, is how. Given the complexity of moral space, given the impossibility of computing the right path, given the certainty of failure—how does one actually navigate toward assembly rather than dissolution?

Recall the insight from Boltzmann and Shannon: when you cannot control the environment, shape the object. In the noisy channel, you cannot eliminate interference—you encode the message. In the jar of nuts, you cannot control every vibration—you let asymmetries do the work. The question is not how to eliminate volatility from your life. The question is what shape you carry into it.

Christ provides both the encoding and the asymmetry. The atonement blocks the absorbing barrier below. The covenants introduce the shape that causes life's volatility to compound toward growth rather than toward spiritual death. But the person must still choose the shape. The person must still, as the Doctrine and Covenants urges, be "anxiously engaged" (D&C 58:27) in the active work of becoming. (EROS)

The sacrament is the recurring encoding ceremony. Weekly, the member of Christ's church takes bread and water and recommits: to take upon them his name, to always remember him, to keep his commandments. These are not sentimental gestures. They are recalibration. They re-establish the redundancy that protects the signal of a person's truest self against the noise of the week. The promise on the Lord's side: "that they may always have his Spirit to be with them" (D&C 20:77). The Spirit is the live feedback loop—the receiver continuously confirming receipt and requesting retransmission where the signal has been corrupted.

And repentance, seen this way, is not the humiliation it can feel like. It is the mechanism by which error is corrected before it compounds—the retransmission of a corrupted symbol. Grace makes retransmission possible. Without grace, the first error condemns. With grace, the first error initiates a cycle of correction and growth. This is why Moroni can say, near the end of the Book of Mormon, with the entire sweep of his civilization's collapse before him, "yea, come unto Christ, and be perfected in him" (Moroni 10:32). Not despite the ruins. Because of what the ruins revealed about the necessity of the encoding. There is a breaking point where things stop compounding towards destruction and begin compounding toward life.

### Eve's Legacy

We return, finally, to Eve.

Eve invoked the final separation—the last broken symmetry that made moral agency possible. But what Eve saw, and what her decision implies, is that separation was never the end. The same boldness that led her to break the symmetry of the Garden is the boldness required to enter covenant. She did not eat the fruit so that mankind could remain scattered in moral confusion. She ate it so that mankind could, through the long process of learning to act rather than merely be acted upon, eventually be assembled into something that could not have existed in the Garden's protected simplicity.

The sealed marriage, the covenant community, the body of Christ—these are Eve's inheritance. They are the assembly phase of the creation she initiated. She broke the symmetry so that, in the fullness of time, Christ could gather all things together in one—not destroying the distinctions the Fall introduced, but bringing them to their intended fruition in a new and higher unity.

The arc of creation is: separation, that there might be agency. Agency, that there might be covenant. Covenant, that there might be assembly. Assembly, that there might be joy.

Adam fell that man might be, and men are, that they might have joy.

Eve perhaps knew this. Before there was a rulebook she looked out at the dimensionality of a moral world—its beauty, its stakes, its staggering potential—and she chose to enter it. She did not compute. She acted from the deepest thing she knew, from what we might call faith, and trusted that something larger would catch what she dropped. It did. It does. It will. We live in a post-Christ world. The atonement has been performed and we live in the energy of its shock wave.

You are already outside the Garden, and you have already eaten the fruit, and the question now is not whether to leave but how to walk. Not with the stiff-legged compliance of a person consulting a map every third step, but with the gathered confidence of someone who has been shaped—by suffering, by grace, by choosing again and again to act rather than be acted upon—into a being whose very movement through the world tends, under all the noise and uncertainty, toward what is good.
